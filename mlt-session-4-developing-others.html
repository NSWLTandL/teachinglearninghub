Session 4 · Self-study

Developing others

Your success is measured by the growth of your team. This area moves from managing to developing — coaching, adult learning, and the art of the stretch opportunity.

“When people know that we care about them and believe in them, they are more open to feedback and more likely to grow.”

The shift

The developer lens

Middle leadership is not just about managing; it is about growing people. Developing others means seeing potential, creating opportunities, and supporting progress. It is a shift from doing to enabling.

Manager lensDeveloper lens
“Are they meeting expectations?”“How can I help them exceed expectations?”
“They need to improve.”“What support or feedback will help them improve?”
“I’ll handle this myself.”“Who could I coach or involve to build capacity?”
“They’re not ready yet.”“What would help them get ready?”
How development works

Coaching: asking, not telling

Effective development usually starts with coaching, not instructing. Coaching builds ownership, reflection, and confidence — the ingredients of sustainable growth.

Jim Knight (Impact Cycle) — coaching is a partnership built on trust: identify a goal collaboratively, learn new strategies together, then reflect and refine through dialogue.
Costa & Garmston (Cognitive Coaching) — questions that promote metacognition make people more self-directed: ‘what led you to that decision?’, ‘what might you try next time?’
Knowles (Adult Learning) — adults learn best when involved in diagnosing their own needs and solving real problems — so coaching is responsive, relevant, and respects autonomy.
Cognitive science — adults learn when they see relevance, experience modelling, and engage in spaced practice; design development that is sequenced, revisited, and embedded.

Coaching starters: ‘What do you think went well?’ · ‘What would you do differently next time?’ · ‘What support would help you move forward?’ · ‘What’s one small change you could try?’

Grow capacity

Spotting and stretching potential

Great leaders notice potential and create stretch opportunities — not random extra tasks, but intentional development aligned to a person’s strengths and goals.

  • Delegate a real responsibility.
  • Ask someone to lead a meeting, CPD session, or project.
  • Suggest CPD, peer observation, or joint planning.
  • Give feedback that nudges growth, then follow up with recognition.
Daniel Pink (Drive) — autonomy, mastery and purpose drive professional growth — good stretch taps all three.
Bandura (self-efficacy) — success in a stretch task grows confidence and capacity, creating a virtuous cycle.
Viviane Robinson — leaders who actively promote and take part in teacher learning have the greatest impact on pupil outcomes.
The best stretch is — aligned to strengths and goals, supported with coaching and feedback, and followed up with reflection and recognition.

Reflect

Make a stretch map

  1. Who in your team has grown most in the last 6–12 months — and what contributed to it?
  2. Who is ready for more right now? What could they lead or try, and what support would they need?
  3. Where are the missed opportunities — potential you have noticed but not yet acted on?
