01 · Why this matters

The last impression is the next first impression.

A class that is dismissed in a controlled, virtue-anchored way leaves the lesson better than they entered. A class that spills out of the room teaches the next teacher a different lesson.

The end of a lesson is the most-overlooked moment in the school day. Many teachers treat the final two minutes as a release valve — the bell goes, books slam, pupils flood the corridor. The standard collapses precisely where it most needs to hold, because the pupils carry whatever happened in those two minutes into the next room, the next class, the next teacher.

This is the bookend of Module 1.2. Where the lesson-start sequence builds the standard, the lesson-end sequence preserves it. The six stages below are smaller in number than the nine of Lesson Starts — and shorter to deliver — but no less precise. Each maps to a specific item on the Lesson Endings checklist (LE1 through LE6).

How a lesson ends shapes what the pupil expects from the next one. Routines that close are the routines that compound. The Teaching Excellence Programme

This module assumes you have completed Modules 1.1 and 1.2. The mantra, the named virtues, and the corridor-handover routine all appear in what follows.

1
The pack-up call

Clear instructions. Enough time.

−2 min before bell
ChecklistLE1LE2

Two minutes before the bell, the teacher pauses the work and gives the pack-up instruction. Two minutes — not thirty seconds, not five. Two is the calibrated time for forty pupils to close books, return resources, and stand behind their chairs without rush.

The instruction itself is short. The teacher does not negotiate the time, and does not narrate the lesson's content during it. The work is done; the close has begun.

The technique
From strong position, hand raised for attention. State the time, name the action, name the standard. Three-step instruction in the order pupils will perform it.
Scripted language"In two minutes, we'll be standing behind chairs. Pens down. Books closed. Equipment away."
Deliberate practice60 seconds · in pairs
  1. One colleague is the teacher; the other plays a class still working.
  2. The teacher delivers the pack-up call from a strong position. Three steps. In order.
  3. Switch.
Coach prompt: Was the time named explicitly? Were the steps in the order pupils would perform them? Did the teacher resist explaining the lesson at this point?
If you have failed LE1 or LE2

The fix is structural: build the two-minute pack-up into your lesson plan as a discrete moment, not a remainder. Set a recurring timer for the first two weeks until it becomes habit.

2
Stand behind chairs

Silent. Still. Ready.

−30 sec
ChecklistLE3

Thirty seconds before the bell, pupils are standing behind their chairs. Silent. Still. The room reverses what the lesson start built — pupils transition from academic back to social — but on the teacher's terms, not the bell's.

This is the lesson-end equivalent of the 30-Second Reflection. The silence is the standard. Without it, every subsequent stage degrades.

The technique
Strong position. Pause. Scan. Wait until every pupil is standing, silent, still. Do not begin Stage 3 until the room has fully arrived. If there is chatter, hold the pause.
Scripted language"Stand behind chairs. — Peacefulness, please."
Deliberate practice90 seconds · in trios
  1. One colleague is the teacher; two are pupils — one compliant, one slow to settle.
  2. The teacher delivers the line and waits. Holds the pause. Scans. Resumes only when both pupils are silent.
  3. Rotate roles. Repeat.
Coach prompt: Did the teacher actually wait, or did they push on through chatter? Was the silence held long enough to register?
If you have failed LE3

This is the foundation stage. Repeat the trio practice daily for a week. If the issue is consistent across classes, review your in-class peacefulness routine in Module 1.7.

3
The virtue close

Name the virtue the class showed.

−20 sec
ChecklistLE3Module 1.1

From the silence, the teacher closes the lesson with one short sentence. The same form every lesson: thank the class, name the virtue they showed, signal the next encounter.

This is the moment Module 1.1 set up. The vocabulary of virtues is delivered last so that pupils carry the named virtue out of the room with them. It anchors the next lesson before the next lesson has begun.

The technique
One sentence. Three parts: thanks, virtue named, sign-off. No mini-recap of the lesson. No homework reminders here — those go before the pack-up call. The close is the close.
Scripted language"Thank you, [Class]. That was a lesson of real determination. I'll see you tomorrow."
Deliberate practice60 seconds · in pairs
  1. One colleague is the teacher; the other is the class, standing silently.
  2. The teacher delivers the virtue close. One sentence. Three parts.
  3. Repeat with three different virtues — diligence, peacefulness, determination — and three different sign-offs.
Coach prompt: Was the virtue specific to what the class actually showed today, or was it generic? Did the close feel warm, or was it perfunctory?
4
Prayer · Conditional

Prayer at end of period 4 and end of day.

−15 sec
ChecklistLE5
For Nishkam School only

This stage is part of the Nishkam School routine at the end of period 4 and at the end of the day. It is conditional and does not run after every lesson.

For other schools: omit this stage entirely, or substitute with your school's equivalent end-of-day or end-of-block ritual.

When the stage runs, prayer is completed fully and with head coverings. The teacher leads quietly; pupils are silent and still throughout. The dismissal in Stage 5 follows immediately after.

The technique
Head coverings on before the prayer begins, not during. The teacher waits for the room to be ready before starting. Pupils remain standing behind chairs throughout. No dismissal until the prayer is complete.
5
Row-by-row dismissal

Silent. Deliberate. In order.

0 / +20 sec
ChecklistLE4

The bell does not dismiss the class. The teacher does. Pupils depart row by row, in silence, in the order the teacher names them.

The order matters. The same row is not always first. Vary it deliberately so pupils do not pre-position themselves to be released. The teacher decides; the class waits.

The technique
Name the row. Pause. Watch the row leave. Name the next row only when the previous one is fully through the door. Do not rush. Do not allow overlap.
Scripted language"Row 3, you may leave. — Pause. — Row 1. — Pause. — Row 4. — Pause. — Row 2."
Deliberate practice90 seconds · in quartets
  1. Four colleagues form four "rows" — chairs in a line, each colleague standing behind one.
  2. One additional colleague is the teacher.
  3. The teacher dismisses the rows in a non-obvious order, holding the pause between each.
  4. The "rows" walk slowly past the teacher to the door, in silence.
Coach prompt: Were the pauses long enough that one row was clear before the next was named? Did the teacher vary the order, or default to row 1 first?
If you have failed LE4

The most common cause is rushing the dismissal because the bell has gone. Practise holding three full seconds between rows. Use a stopwatch in the next three lessons.

6
Corridor handover

Door, then corridor. Welcome the next.

+30 sec
ChecklistLE6

Once the last row has left, the teacher moves to the door, then into the corridor. This is not optional. The corridor presence at the start of the next teacher's lesson is built by the corridor presence at the end of yours.

The teacher reinforces single file, left-hand side, by being there. The first pupils of the next class are already arriving. Greet them. The lesson cycle continues.

The technique
The door is the threshold of the routine. Stand there, body angled to see both the room emptying and the corridor filling. As the last departing pupil clears the door, step into the corridor. Speak to incoming pupils. Begin Stage 1 of Module 1.2 for the next class.
Scripted language"Single file, left-hand side, please. — Morning, [Pupil]."
If you have failed LE6

This stage is the bridge between your lesson and the next teacher's. The fix is structural — build the corridor handover into your lesson timing so you are not still tidying when the next class arrives.

03 · End-to-end practice

Now run the whole sequence.

You have rehearsed each stage in isolation. The final practice is the whole close, end to end, in real time.

FEATURED PRACTICE · 5 MINUTES · IN QUARTETS

The full ninety-second close.

One quartet, one teacher, one observer, two pupils standing behind imaginary chairs. Run the sequence from pack-up call to corridor handover — without stopping. The observer holds the LE checklist open and ticks each item as it lands.

  1. Reset: the "class" is mid-task. The clock is two minutes from the bell.
  2. Run: teacher executes Stages 1–3, then Stage 5, then Stage 6. (Stage 4 is omitted in standard practice; run it separately if your context requires it.)
  3. Debrief: observer reads back which LE items landed and which were rushed or skipped. Two minutes only.
  4. Rotate and run again. The second attempt is always more secure than the first.
Coach prompt: Was the silence held in Stage 2? Was the dismissal varied in order? Did the teacher actually move into the corridor for Stage 6, or did they stop at the doorway?
04 · Common pitfalls

Where lesson endings collapse.

Each pitfall is tagged with the checklist item it puts at risk.

The bell dismisses the class.Risks LE4

Pupils stand on the bell, sometimes before the bell. The teacher dismisses the class — never the bell. If pupils start moving before being named, return to Stage 2.

The rush.Risks LE2, LE3

Thirty seconds becomes ten. Two minutes becomes one. The pack-up is squeezed because the lesson ran long. The pack-up is the final routine, not the leftover time.

The vague close.Risks LE3

"Right, see you next time." No virtue named. No anchor for the next lesson. The vocabulary that ran through the lesson is dropped in the final ten seconds.

The teacher stays at the desk.Risks LE6

The class leaves; the teacher heads back to the computer to log behaviour points. The corridor is unsupervised. The next lesson begins without a corridor presence.

The same row first, every time.Risks LE4

Pupils on the front row pack up early because they know they go first. The order is a routine; vary it deliberately.

Homework as the final word.Risks LE3

"Don't forget your homework's due Tuesday." The lesson closes on logistics rather than on a virtue. Move homework reminders to before the pack-up call.

06 · AI scenario tester

A real lesson-end moment.

The full live tool will accept any scenario you describe. The worked example below shows the shape of the response.

Worked example

Scenario tester

Teacher submits
"My [Year 10] lesson ran over. I had three pupils still doing the final question when the bell went. The rest of the class started packing up themselves. By the time I'd told the three to stop, half the class was already at the door. I gave up and let them go."
Tool responds

Items at risk: LE1 (no clear pack-up call given), LE2 (insufficient time), LE3 (pupils were not silent behind chairs), LE4 (pupils dismissed themselves rather than row-by-row).

What happened: the lesson ran over and the close was sacrificed for content. This is the most common failure mode at lesson end. The four items collapsed in sequence the moment the pack-up call was skipped.

Triage for next lesson:

  1. Set a phone timer for two minutes before the bell. When it goes, the lesson stops — even if pupils are mid-sentence.
  2. Deliver the pack-up call from strong position. Three steps: "Pens down. Books closed. Equipment away. Two minutes."
  3. At thirty seconds: "Stand behind chairs. Peacefulness."
  4. Hold the silence. If pupils chatter, wait — even if it eats into the dismissal time.
  5. Virtue close. One sentence.
  6. Row-by-row dismissal — even one second of pause between rows is enough.

The longer-term fix: if your lessons consistently run over, the issue is not the close — it is the lesson structure. Build the close into your timing as a fixed two-minute block, not a remainder. Many teachers find it helps to write "STOP" on their lesson plan at the two-minute mark.

What not to do: do not extend the lesson by another minute "just to finish this question." The completed close compounds across the year; the unfinished question does not.
