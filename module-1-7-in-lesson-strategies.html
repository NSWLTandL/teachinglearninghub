In-Lesson Strategies.

The pre-warning territory. Five graduated interventions to use before reaching for the warning ladder — quietest first, loudest last, and almost never the loudest.

01 · Why this matters

The territory between routine and warning.

Module 1.3 taught the everyday Diligence Routine. Module 1.8 will teach the formal warning ladder. This module sits in the gap between them — the pre-warning interventions a skilled teacher reaches for ten or fifteen times per lesson, often without anyone noticing.

Behaviour does not collapse all at once. Most lessons drift before they fall. Two pupils start a side conversation, the volume of the room ticks up half a notch, a third pupil turns to look. None of this is a behaviour incident; all of it is a signal. The skilled teacher reads those signals and intervenes early, with the lightest touch the situation will allow. The unskilled teacher waits until the room has demonstrably tipped, then reaches for the heaviest tool available — and teaches the room that low-level drift is acceptable until things get loud.

The strategy ladder below is graduated. Each rung is heavier and more public than the one above. The principle is simple: start at the top of the ladder, descend only as far as the situation requires, and return to the top as soon as the room allows.

The teacher who rarely raises their voice has not simply mastered self-control. That teacher has rehearsed the quieter moves that come first, and reaches the spoken intervention only when the earlier rungs have not resolved the situation.

This module assumes completion of Module 1.3 (the Diligence Routine). Several rungs of the ladder reference techniques covered there: strong position, the named virtues, and the pause-and-scan. Where those phrases are unfamiliar, Module 1.3 is the prerequisite.

02 · The strategy ladder

Five rungs. Top-down.

The ladder is read as a descent. The lightest touch sits at the top, and the teacher descends further only as far as the situation requires.

↓ INCREASING WEIGHT & VISIBILITY ↓
1

Positioning BFL9

Anticipated through where the teacher stands. The lightest touch — silent, invisible, often pre-emptive.
2

Proximity BFL9

Move closer to the issue without speaking. The pupil notices; nobody else does.
3

Eye-meet BFL9

Silent acknowledgement. The pupil registers that the behaviour has been seen.
4

Strong-position reset BFL8

Return to centre. Pause. The whole room reads the change. Still no words.
5

Named virtue BFL8

"Peacefulness" or "diligence". The teacher has now spoken — but names only the virtue, not the pupil.

If the room has not reset by Rung 5, the next move is the formal warning ladder (Module 1.8). Rung 5 is the boundary of pre-warning territory. Cross it deliberately.

1
Rung 1 · Positioning

Anticipation through position.

lightest touch
ChecklistBFL9

Positioning is the strategy the teacher uses before anything has gone wrong. The teacher who knows the class knows where the issues will start, and stands near them — not over them, not pointedly close, but in a position from which a glance or a step is enough. The pupil who would have started a conversation does not, because the teacher is already there.

Most teachers default to the front of the room. Skilled teachers default to wherever the next problem is likely to come from.

When to use
Always. This is the resting state, not an intervention. For every minute the lesson is running, the teacher is positioned somewhere; positioning is the question of whether that somewhere is deliberate.
The technique
The teacher maps the room before the lesson, identifying two or three points from which most of the class is in eyeline. As pupils enter, the teacher takes one of those positions, and moves between them deliberately as the lesson unfolds — changing position with the lesson's transitions, not in response to incidents.
Deliberate practice5 minutes · individual
  1. Sketch the layout of the most-difficult class on paper.
  2. Mark three positions from which at least 80% of the class is visible without turning.
  3. Plan the transitions: position A for the start, position B for independent work, position C for the close. Record them in the lesson plan.
  4. Run the next lesson from those three positions and note what changes.
Coach prompt: Did the teacher stand in the same place for most of the lesson, or did they move with deliberate intent? Were they near the issues, or near the lectern?
2
Rung 2 · Proximity

Moving closer without speaking.

silent · directed
ChecklistBFL9

When two pupils start a side conversation, the natural reaction is to name them. The lightest move is instead to walk towards them. As the teacher approaches, the conversation almost always stops on its own. The teacher has intervened without announcing the intervention, and the pupils are corrected without an audience.

Crucially, the teacher does not stop near them, but walks past, continuing the circulation. The proximity has done its work; making eye contact or speaking would escalate the rung unnecessarily.

When to use
At the first sign of a side conversation, an off-task gesture, or the start of distraction — earlier than feels comfortable.
The technique
The teacher moves at a normal pace, without looking at the pupils, passing close enough that they register the teacher's presence — usually within two metres — and continues walking. The whole intervention is over in five seconds.
Deliberate practice3 minutes · in trios
  1. Two colleagues sit at a table, half-engaged in a quiet "off-task" conversation.
  2. The teacher walks towards them at normal pace, passes within two metres without looking, and continues.
  3. The "pupils" report: did the conversation stop on its own? At what point?
Coach prompt: Did the teacher stop near the pupils, or pass through? Was the body language neutral, or did it telegraph "I am coming for you"?
3
Rung 3 · Eye-meet

Silent acknowledgement.

silent · directed
ChecklistBFL9

Where proximity alone has not corrected the issue, or where the teacher cannot easily move to the pupil, the next rung is to meet the pupil's eye. This is a held gaze of one or two seconds, followed by looking away — neither a stare nor a glare. The pupil registers that the behaviour has been seen.

The eye-meet is more public than proximity but still silent. Other pupils may notice it; they do not hear anything. The intervention remains localised.

When to use
When proximity is not possible — the other side of the room, mid-instruction, in the middle of modelling — or when proximity has not landed and the pupil needs to register that the behaviour has been noticed.
The technique
The teacher finds the pupil's eyes and holds for one to two seconds. A small nod or a raised eyebrow may accompany it but is not necessary. The teacher then looks away, back to the room or the lesson. The eye-meet is brief; sustained staring escalates the rung.
Deliberate practice3 minutes · in trios
  1. Two colleagues stand at distance as "off-task" pupils.
  2. The teacher continues a "lesson" while making one eye-meet with each of them in turn.
  3. The "pupils" report: did they feel the meet, or was it ambiguous? Was it long enough to register, short enough not to feel hostile?
Coach prompt: Was the eye-meet held, or fleeting? Was it ambiguous, or unmistakable? Did the teacher's face soften or harden during it — and which would the pupil have felt?
4
Rung 4 · Strong-position reset

Return to centre, and pause.

visible · whole-class
ChecklistBFL8

Now the intervention is a whole-class signal, not a directed one. The teacher returns to the strong position — front-centre — and pauses, deliberately, for two or three seconds. No words. The body language alone tells the room that something has shifted.

This rung is the bridge between the silent, individual rungs above and the spoken intervention of Rung 5. Most experienced teachers find that Rung 4 alone resolves the situation about half the time; the room reads the strong-position pause and self-corrects without needing to hear a word.

When to use
When the volume of the room has risen, or when multiple pockets of off-task behaviour are visible at once. The strategy that targets the room rather than a single pupil.
The technique
Walk back to strong position with normal pace — not stalking, not theatrical. Stop. Body upright, hands free, weight even. Hold the pause. Scan once across the room. Most of the class will have reset by the end of the scan.
Deliberate practice3 minutes · in pairs
  1. Walk to a fixed point in the room (the "strong position"), stop deliberately, and hold for three seconds while scanning.
  2. Partner observes: did the body settle into stillness, or did it fidget? Did the scan look like a check or a search?
  3. Repeat three times. The third time should feel anchored.
Coach prompt: Was the strong position genuinely strong — settled, still — or was it a destination the teacher arrived at, then immediately moved on from?
Where BFL8 is rated red or amber (reset half)

The fix is body, not voice. The teacher practises the strong-position pause in front of a mirror — three seconds, no movement. The skill is the stillness, not the position.

5
Rung 5 · Named virtue

"Peacefulness" or "diligence".

spoken · whole-class
ChecklistBFL8

The fifth rung — and the boundary of pre-warning territory — is to speak. But only the named virtue, not the pupil and not the behaviour. From the strong position, after the held pause, the teacher names what the room needs to return to: peacefulness if the room needs calming, diligence if it needs focusing.

Critically, the teacher does not say "be quiet", "stop talking", or "[Pupil], I'm waiting". Each of those phrasings names the failure rather than the standard. The named virtue does the opposite: it tells the room what to be, not what to stop being.

When to use
When Rung 4 has not landed. Or when the room is too disordered for non-verbal cues to register. Or — rarely — straight from the start of a transition that requires immediate calm.
The technique
From the strong position, after the pause and scan, at normal volume: one word. The teacher then holds the silence and waits, exactly as in the Diligence Routine. The trigger word is the same; what differs is that the teacher has arrived here from Rungs 1–4 rather than from a planned routine.
Scripted language"Peacefulness." (or) "Diligence."
Deliberate practice3 minutes · in pairs
  1. Partner generates a fictitious scenario ("The room is moderately noisy after a paired discussion") and the teacher names which virtue would fit.
  2. The teacher then runs the full sequence: walk to strong position, pause, scan, deliver the named virtue.
  3. Repeat with three different scenarios — one where peacefulness is the right call, one where diligence is, one ambiguous.
Coach prompt: Was the choice of virtue right for the situation? Did the teacher pause and scan before speaking, or rush to the word? Did they say only the virtue, or did they qualify it ("peacefulness — settle down please")?
Where BFL8 is rated red or amber (virtue half)

The choice is planned in advance. Before each transition, the teacher decides which virtue the room is most likely to need next. Delivering it becomes easier when the choice has not had to be made under pressure.

03 · Reading the room

Choosing the right rung.

The ladder is graduated, but choosing which rung to start from is a judgment call. Three principles guide the choice.

FEATURED PRACTICE · 5 MINUTES · IN TRIOS

Scenario triage.

This is the practice that turns the ladder from theory into instinct.

  1. Set: the trio agrees five plausible mid-lesson scenarios — one side conversation, one phone glanced at, one pupil staring out of the window, one rising room-volume, one pupil arriving late and disrupting.
  2. Run: for each scenario, the teacher names the rung to start on, and why. The other two challenge the choice — could it have started higher?
  3. Score: for each scenario, record the rung chosen. Look at the spread. Most teachers under pressure default to Rungs 4 or 5 for everything; the goal is to be on Rung 1 or 2 for at least three of the five.
  4. Rotate.

Three guiding principles:

  1. Start at the top. Default to the lightest touch. Descend only as far as needed.
  2. Return to the top as soon as the room allows. Once the situation has reset, the teacher returns to Rung 1 (positioning) rather than staying at the rung the situation required.
  3. The room reads how it is read. The pupils watch how the teacher intervenes. Light, early, frequent interventions teach the room that drift gets noticed; heavy, late, rare interventions teach the room that drift is acceptable until it is loud.
Coach prompt: Across the five scenarios, what was the average rung chosen? Was there one scenario where you instinctively went to Rung 5 — and could you have started higher?
04 · Common pitfalls

Where the ladder gets misused.

Each pitfall is tagged with the checklist item it puts at risk.

Defaulting to Rung 5.Risks BFL9, BFL-O2

Every intervention is the named virtue. The teacher has technically done the right thing on each occasion, but they have not used Rungs 1–4 at all. The room learns that drift goes unnoticed until the volume rises.

Skipping straight to Module 1.8.Risks BFL8

Naming a pupil and giving a warning when the situation could have been resolved by proximity or eye-meet. The warning ladder is for behaviour that has already crossed the line; this module is for stopping it crossing.

Static positioning.Risks BFL9

The teacher stands at the front for the whole lesson, then wonders why the back row is the issue. Position changes with lesson phase, deliberately.

Stopping at the issue during proximity.Risks BFL9

The teacher walks to the chatty table, then stands over it. The intervention has now become public. Proximity is a pass-through, not a perch.

Naming the failure rather than the standard.Risks BFL8

"Stop talking" instead of "peacefulness". The named-failure phrasing teaches what to avoid; the named-virtue phrasing teaches what to do. Same volume, different lesson.

The hostile eye-meet.Risks BFL9

The eye-meet held too long, with hardened face. Now an intervention has become a confrontation. One to two seconds, neutral face, then look away.

Climbing up after the descent.Risks BFL9

The teacher reached Rung 4 to reset the room — then stays in strong position for the rest of the lesson. The room registers that they have "won" something, and the next intervention has to start from a higher rung.

05 · Self-audit · BFL8, BFL9

The canonical checklist.

OUTCOME ITEM — DOWNSTREAM SIGNAL

As in Module 1.3, BFL-O2 is the downstream signal. The action items measure the teacher's technique; the outcome item measures the effect. A teacher who scores green on BFL8 and BFL9 but amber on BFL-O2 is using the ladder competently, but something else is dragging focus — likely cognitive load or task design (Pillars 3 and 4).

06 · AI scenario tester

A real in-lesson moment.

The full live tool will accept any scenario you describe. The worked example below shows the shape of the response.

Worked example

Scenario tester

Teacher submits
"It's twenty minutes into a [Year 9] lesson. They're working on a paired task. I can hear two boys at the back having a different conversation — not loud, but clearly off-topic. I named them across the room: '[Pupil], [Pupil] — focus please.' They went quiet for thirty seconds, then started again. I had to name them a second time. I'm worried I'm going to have to give them a warning if it carries on."
Tool responds

Items at risk: BFL9 (positioning and proximity not used as primary management — you went straight to a Rung 5-equivalent technique, the named-and-public callout). BFL8 (the named-virtue technique was misused: you named the pupils, not the virtue).

What happened: the named-and-public callout is below Rung 5 on the strategy ladder — it is a form of warning rather than a pre-warning intervention. You started below the bottom rung and skipped the four rungs above it. The pupils heard a warning-level intervention and responded for thirty seconds; the next time, the same intervention had less weight because it had been used twice.

What to do next time:

  1. The first time you hear the off-topic conversation: Rung 1 (positioning). Move to a position that puts you in eyeline of the back row. No words. No look.
  2. If the conversation continues 30 seconds after you've moved: Rung 2 (proximity). Walk past their table without stopping. Most of the time, this alone resolves it.
  3. If it persists: Rung 3 (eye-meet). As you continue circulating, find their eyes briefly. One to two seconds. Then move on.
  4. If the volume of the room has risen too: Rung 4 (strong-position reset). Walk back to centre, pause, scan. The whole class will read it.
  5. Only then, if needed: Rung 5 (named virtue). From strong position, after the pause, say "peacefulness" — not "[Pupil], focus please".

The longer-term fix: when you find yourself naming pupils in front of the class, that is almost always a signal that the four lighter rungs have been skipped. Coach yourself by counting how many times in a lesson you reach the named-pupil intervention. Aim to halve it next lesson.

What not to do: do not skip straight to Module 1.8 (the warning ladder) when the issue is still in pre-warning territory. The warning ladder is the consequence system; this module is what you do to avoid needing it.
