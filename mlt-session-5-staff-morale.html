Session 5 · Self-study

Staff morale

Morale is shaped by what a leader does. This area covers what really drives it — Maslow, Herzberg, transformational leadership — and the everyday behaviours that lift a team or drain it.

“When staff feel inspired, valued, and supported, they are more engaged, motivated, and committed.”

Why it matters

Morale is a leadership behaviour

Morale is not a mood that happens to a team; it is shaped, day to day, by how a leader behaves. Three ideas explain what drives it.

Maslow · Hierarchy of needs

People are motivated through levels of need, from the basic upward.

What it means for a team

  • Safety: security, stability, fair treatment.
  • Belonging: connection and a sense of being part of the team.
  • Esteem: recognition, respect, achievement.
  • Lower needs must be reasonably met before people reach for innovation.

Herzberg · Two factors

Hygiene factors and motivators do different jobs.

What it means for a team

  • Hygiene (pay, policy, conditions): their absence causes dissatisfaction.
  • Fixing hygiene removes dissatisfaction — but does not motivate.
  • Motivators (achievement, recognition, responsibility, growth) drive engagement.
  • So: clear the irritants, then invest in the motivators.

Transformational leadership

Lift morale and performance through vision and trust, not just task management.

What it means for a team

  • Communicate a clear, compelling purpose people can see themselves in.
  • Model integrity, enthusiasm and commitment.
  • Recognise strengths; coach and grow individuals.
  • Make it safe to share ideas and take initiative.
In your team

What lifts morale, and what drains it

Drawing the theory together: most of what moves morale is within a middle leader’s gift.

Lifts morale

  • Being noticed: specific, genuine recognition.
  • Responsibility and trust to do the work well.
  • Growth: stretch, CPD, a path forward.
  • Belonging: a team that feels like a team.
  • Fairness and consistency you can rely on.
  • A clear, shared purpose worth the effort.

Drains morale

  • Effort that goes unseen or unacknowledged.
  • Inconsistency and perceived unfairness.
  • Workload with no control or no let-up.
  • Being managed but never developed.
  • Mixed messages and shifting priorities.
  • Cynicism left to spread unchallenged.
The Nishkam frame

Optimism and joy, on purpose

Two of our four leadership ideas speak directly to morale — and they are choices a leader makes, not weather a team waits out.

We approach life with optimism. Positivity is a discipline that builds momentum, especially when things are hard.
We look for joy. Joy in the work and the relationships is contagious, and it builds resilient teams.
We work for others. Attending to a colleague’s wellbeing is service, not a distraction from the ‘real’ work.
We consider others. Noticing the person behind the role is where morale begins.
