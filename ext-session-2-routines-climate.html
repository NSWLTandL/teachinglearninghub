Routines & Climate.

A calm, purposeful room is built from a few well-taught routines. This session covers which ones are worth the effort, how we teach them so they hold, and a calm way to deal with behaviour when it slips — our routines, with our own wording taken out.

Starting here

A calm room is something you build.

Some classes feel orderly and some feel like hard work, and it is easy to put the difference down to the pupils. Far more of it comes down to routines — the small, repeated procedures that mean nobody has to think about how the lesson runs, so everyone can think about the work instead. This session sets out the routines we have found worth building, the way we teach them so they hold, and a calm way of dealing with behaviour when it slips.

A routine is just a process the class knows by heart: how to come in, how to start, what to do when they are stuck, how to pack away. Once it is established it costs almost nothing to run — you name it and the class does it — and the energy you would otherwise spend managing the room is freed up for teaching. The catch is that the cost is paid up front. A routine has to be taught, practised, and insisted on for a couple of weeks before it runs itself. As with Session 1, the routines below are ours with the school-specific wording taken out, so you can put your own in.

The routines

Five routines worth building.

Start with the ones your classes most need. Each follows the same rule: the same words and the same steps, every time. We have set them out step by step, because that is how they are taught — not described to a class once, but modelled and rehearsed.

The same start every time.

Decide what happens in the first minute or two — where pupils sit, what they take out, what they start — and keep it identical every lesson. A class that knows exactly how to begin begins without being told. Have the first task on the board before the bell, so coming in and starting are the same thing. Set the entry expectation out loud as they arrive, in the same words each day.

How to teach it: For the first two weeks, narrate the entry every lesson — "[your settle cue], coats off, planner out, start the task on the board" — using the same words until it runs without you.

One signal to bring them back.

Choose a single, consistent way of calling the room back to you — a phrase, a raised hand, a countdown — and use only that one. Mixing signals teaches a class to wait and see which one you mean this time. One signal, taught properly and insisted on, brings a room back in a few seconds.

How to teach it: Teach the signal openly. "When I do this, you stop, look this way, and wait — within five seconds." Then practise it cold, twice, before you need it for real.

Short, numbered instructions.

Give a task as a tight sequence the class can hold — "one, do this; two, then this; three, then check it." Count the steps on your fingers as you say them. The test is whether a pupil could repeat it back to you. A long, vague instruction gets half-followed; a short numbered one gets done. This is the same discipline as the five-word instruction from Session 1, applied to a task rather than a single command.

How to teach it: Before you set a task, give it in three numbered steps, then cold-call one pupil to say step two back. If they can't, the instruction was too long.

A known way to get unstuck.

Teach the class what to do before they put a hand up — re-read the question, check the example, try the first line. A routine for being stuck cuts the queue at your desk and builds a bit of independence, and the hands that are still up afterwards are the ones that genuinely need you. It needs teaching like any other routine; pupils don't arrive knowing it.

How to teach it: Put a three-step "stuck?" prompt on the wall. When a pupil says "I don't get it," point to it rather than answering, until checking it first becomes the habit.

A clean ending.

End the lesson on your terms rather than the bell's. A consistent close — pack away on the signal, a last check, leave by table when the room is settled — means the lesson finishes in order instead of dissolving into the corridor. The last thirty seconds colour how the class remembers the whole hour, and a scrambled ending undoes a good lesson.

How to teach it: Build a two-step pack-away on a single signal, leave a couple of minutes for it, and only let the class leave when the room is still and ready.
When behaviour slips

A calm ladder.

Even in a well-run room, low-level disruption happens. The aim is to deal with it early and quietly, before it grows, and without your own manner changing. A simple ladder does the job: a non-verbal cue first — a look, standing nearby, a pause beside the pupil — then a quiet private word, then a clear named consequence if it carries on. Each rung is calm and matter-of-fact. You are applying something everyone knows, not winning a contest.

Most disruption stops at the first or second rung — a glance, or simply standing nearby — precisely because you have dealt with it early and without fuss. Going straight to a consequence escalates a moment that standing next to the pupil would have settled on its own.
Where it tends to go wrong

Three things to watch.

Changing the words each time.

If the instruction or the signal is worded differently every lesson, it isn't a routine — it's a fresh instruction the class has to work out each time. Settle on the words and keep them.

Teaching it once and assuming it.

A routine introduced on Monday and taken for granted by Wednesday won't hold. It is taught across two weeks of daily repetition. The patience now is what buys you the automatic version later.

Letting it slide when you're tired.

The first time you walk past a broken routine, the class learns it is optional. Standards are held by what you actually insist on at the end of a long week, not by what you meant to do.

The checklist

What a colleague would look for.

The same self-audit we use for lesson starts and endings, with our own wording generalised. Rate each red, amber, or green.

R1 · The teacher is at the door as the lesson begins.

You are at the threshold as pupils arrive, setting the tone before they sit down.

R2 · Entry expectations are set out loud as pupils come in.

You narrate how to come in, in the same words each day, until it no longer needs saying.

R3 · Pupils start the first task straight away.

A task is waiting that needs no explanation, and the class gets on with it on arrival.

R4 · Any opening routine is observed quietly and settled.

Whatever your settled start looks like, it happens calmly and without prompting.

R5 · The register is taken quietly while pupils work.

Administration happens in the background; it doesn't interrupt the start.

R6 · Instructions during transitions are clear and given into a settled room.

Moves between activities are directed clearly, not shouted over a busy class.

R7 · Clear instructions to pack up are given, with time to do it.

The end is signalled clearly, with a couple of minutes set aside so it isn't a scramble.

R8 · Pupils leave in an orderly way, when the room is settled.

The class departs in order — by row or table — rather than spilling out at the bell.

R9 · The teacher goes to the corridor to support the transition out.

You see the class out and help keep the corridor calm between lessons.

Coaching task

Write one routine out properly.

15 minutes · in pairs

From vague to scripted.

  1. Each pick one routine your classes currently do inconsistently.
  2. Write it out as exact steps with exact words — what you say, what they do, in order.
  3. Read it to your partner. They flag any step a brand-new pupil couldn't follow.
  4. Agree how you'll teach it: model it, practise it cold, and insist on it daily for two weeks.
