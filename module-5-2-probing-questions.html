Orientation

Five routines, one purpose.

This module covers probing — the follow-up that develops the first answer into deeper thinking. Probing typically follows cold-calling (Module 5.1) or a mini-whiteboard reveal (Module 4.1); the answer itself is the starting point, not the destination.

RoutineUse it whenLooks likeModule

01 · Why this matters

Probing is building, not guessing.

The first answer is rarely the deepest answer. A pupil who has retrieved "prophase" has correctly named the first phase of mitosis but has not yet shown how they know it, why it comes first, or what would change if it did not. Probing develops the surface answer into the underlying reasoning.

Dylan Wiliam's five strategies for questioning shape the technique: ask fewer, better questions; build in wait time; use follow-ups (why, can you explain, what do you mean by); check understanding with the class; use error as opportunity for further thinking. The probing sequence below is how those five principles operate together in a single cold-call follow-up.

Classroom questioning works best when it leads to thinking — not just answers. The goal isn't getting it right — it's figuring it out.Dylan Wiliam

The probing sequence is delivered after a first answer has been received. It is not a separate routine; it is what cold-calling and mini-whiteboards open into. Without probing, both routines stop at the surface answer and the deeper thinking never arrives.

1
Step 1 · Open question

An open question. Asked of the whole class first.

PQ1PQ2

Probing begins with an open question — one that admits more than one valid response or requires reasoning rather than recall. The question is asked of the whole class first, with held thinking time, before any pupil is selected. PQ2 is explicit: cold-calling or an alternative selection technique chooses the pupil after the pause.

Open phrasing matters. "What was the cause of the First World War?" admits one rote answer; "What were the conditions in Europe that made war possible by 1914?" admits several lines of reasoning and rewards the kind of thinking the probing sequence is designed to develop.

Scripted language"What were the conditions that made war possible by 1914?" PAUSE · 10 SEC "[Pupil]."
2
Step 2 · First response

Acknowledge. Rephrase if needed.

PQ3

The first answer is acknowledged briefly. Where the answer is partial or unclear, the teacher rephrases it back to the class — "So you're saying the alliance system is what made it possible" — to capture the key idea precisely. The rephrasing is a teaching act: it gives the class a clearer version of the pupil's thinking to work with.

The teacher does not evaluate the answer at this stage. "Good — alliances" closes the thought; "So you're saying the alliance system" opens it for probing in Step 3.

Hold the evaluation. A graded answer is a finished answer. The pupil has been told the thinking is over, and the probing sequence stops here.
3
Step 3 · The probe · the technique

Why? How? Evidence? Alternative?

PQ4

The probe is one short follow-up question that asks the pupil to extend the answer in a specific direction: clarify, justify, provide evidence, consider an alternative, or review the implication. PQ4 names these five probe directions explicitly. The pupil's answer is the input; the probe builds on it rather than restarting.

One probe at a time. Two probes asked together overload the pupil and produce a partial response to one of them. The teacher asks one question, holds the pause, hears the response, and decides whether to probe further before the next question.

Scripted language — one of:"Why is that?" / "How do you know?" / "What's your evidence?" / "What if it were the opposite?" / "What would that mean for…?" PAUSE
Hold the pause again. The probe needs the same wait time as the original question. Higher-order thinking takes ten seconds at minimum; the teacher does not fill the silence.
Coaching moment5 minutes · in pairs
  1. Partner A asks a content-based question on a topic both know.
  2. Partner B answers in one sentence.
  3. Partner A asks two probing questions in sequence — pausing after each, allowing a full response, before moving to the second.
  4. Swap roles.
Coach prompt: Did each probe build on the previous answer, or did it restart the question? Did the teacher hold the pause after each probe?
4
Step 4 · Build

Invite other pupils to extend.

PQ7

Probing is not confined to the original pupil. PQ7 specifies that other pupils are invited to respond to the probing question, to the original pupil's answer, or to extend the line of reasoning. The room is scanned (PQ6) so that the second pupil is selected by name from the same cold-call discipline rather than by hands-up.

The build serves two purposes. It distributes the thinking across the class so the original pupil is not alone in carrying the depth. And it produces multiple lines of reasoning on the same question, which the teacher then either reconciles or holds open for further probing.

Scripted language"[Second pupil] — do you agree with that? PAUSE Why?"
or
"[Third pupil] — what would you add?"
5
Step 5 · Surface new learning

Repeat until something new arrives.

PQ10

The probing sequence is repeated — return to the whole class with a re-asked or adjusted question (PQ8), check understanding by reviewing the answers (PQ9), and continue the cycle until "new learning has surfaced" (PQ10). New learning is the moment a pupil says something the class did not know at the start of the sequence; the teacher recognises it explicitly and consolidates it before moving on.

Where the class is not arriving at new learning, the diagnosis is usually that the original question was at the wrong cognitive level. The Bloom probe stems below give the teacher a planned route through Remember → Understand → Apply → Analyse → Evaluate → Create when a single line of probing is not getting traction.

Name the new learning when it arrives. "That's interesting — let's hold that. What [Pupil] has just said is that the alliance system was as much about deterrence as about preparation for war." Without the explicit naming, pupils experience probing as continuous questioning rather than as cumulative learning.
03 · Bloom probe stems

Six verbs, six stems.

Bloom's taxonomy provides a planned ladder of probe stems. Each stem extends the pupil's thinking one cognitive step further than the surface answer.

LevelProbe stemUsed to
Remember"What do we know about…?"Retrieve prior content the answer relies on.
Understand"Can you explain why…?"Test whether retrieval is matched by understanding.
Apply"How would you use this in…?"Move the concept into a new context.
Analyse"What are the key differences between…?"Surface comparison, structure, or contrast.
Evaluate"Which of … and … is more effective, and why?"Make a judgement supported by criteria.
Create"What might happen if…?"Generate a new response from the existing material.
Coaching moment · plan a probe ladder10 minutes · individual
  1. Choose a topic from this week's lessons.
  2. Write one question at the Remember level. Predict the answer a typical pupil will give.
  3. Using the predicted answer as the starting point, write three probes that move up the Bloom ladder — one Understand, one Analyse, one Evaluate.
  4. Test the ladder on a colleague before using it in lesson.
Coach prompt: Does each probe build on the previous answer, or does each restart the topic at a higher level?
04 · Stretch It

The technique that turns a correct answer into thinking.

Stretch It is the specific probe used after a correct answer to extend the response further. The pupil has the right answer; the technique is to use that as the starting point for deeper thinking.

Four stretch prompts are routine: "How do you know?" — "Talk me through your thought process." — "Could they have used another method?" — "What's different about this answer compared to others we've seen?" Each prompt opens the pupil's correct answer into the reasoning that produced it.

Stretch It turns a correct answer into a thinking opportunity. The pupil who has the answer is asked to make their reasoning visible, which both deepens their own understanding and gives the rest of the class access to the thinking.Doug Lemov · Teach Like a Champion
Scripted Stretch ItPupil: "Photosynthesis." PAUSE
Teacher: "Good. PAUSE How did you know that one was photosynthesis and not respiration?"
05 · Pitfalls

Where probing breaks.

Each pitfall is tagged with the checklist item it puts at risk.

Probing only when the answer is wrong.Risks PQ4

The teacher accepts a correct answer at face value and probes only the wrong ones. The class learns that being right ends the thinking. Stretch It is the technique that probes the correct answers too.

Closed-question probing.Risks PQ4

"Are you sure?" — closed; produces yes or no. "Why are you sure?" — open; produces reasoning. The probe is open and one-step.

Two probes asked together.Risks PQ4

"Why? And what would you say if I told you the opposite?" — two questions in one delivery. The pupil answers neither well. One probe, one pause, one response.

Probing the same pupil to exhaustion.Risks PQ7

Three probes deep into one pupil's reasoning while twenty-nine others wait. The build (Step 4) distributes the thinking; PQ7 invites other pupils to extend.

Filling the pause after the probe.Risks PQ4

"Why? — well, you might think because…" The teacher has answered the probe themselves. The pupil does not need to think; the class does not need to listen.

Probing without a planned destination.Risks PQ10

Probing for its own sake. The sequence ends with no named new learning, the lesson moves on, and the depth of the conversation evaporates. PQ10 specifies that the cycle continues until new learning surfaces — and that the new learning is named.

07 · AI scenario tester

Worked example.

Worked example of a typical tool response.

Worked example

Scenario tester

Teacher submits
"My probing always sounds like an interrogation. I cold-call, the pupil gives a one-word answer, I ask 'why?' and they freeze. I end up explaining the reasoning myself and moving on. The class doesn't really probe."
Tool responds

Items at risk: PQ1 (the original question may be closed — producing a one-word answer is a sign), PQ3 (no rephrase before the probe), PQ4 (the "why?" is unsupported by Step 2's rephrase), PQ7 (no build to other pupils when the first pupil freezes).

What is happening: the cold-call has produced a recall answer; the probe is asking for higher-order thinking on top of recall, with no scaffold between the two. The pupil cannot bridge the gap and the teacher fills the silence to rescue them.

Across the next two weeks:

  1. Open the original question. "What is X?" produces one word; "What does X mean here?" produces a sentence. The probe builds on a sentence, not on a word.
  2. Insert Step 2. After the pupil's answer, rephrase it back: "So you're saying X means Y." The rephrase gives the pupil their own thinking back as a foothold for the probe.
  3. Build, don't only probe. When the first pupil freezes, move to PQ7 — invite a second pupil to extend, then return to the first with the second pupil's answer as material to react to.
  4. Plan one probe ladder per lesson. Choose one concept, write the open question and three probes in advance, run the ladder live. The success rate of planned probes is several times that of improvised probes.

To avoid: filling the silence yourself. The freeze is information — the pupil is signalling that the gap between the answer and the probe is too large. Rephrase, build to another pupil, or step back to a shallower probe.
