Reading for Pleasure.

A reading culture is built, not hoped for: the visible, everyday practices that make a school one where pupils read widely and willingly, and where adults model being readers.

Orientation

A culture, built deliberately.

Most of these practices are the visible scaffolding of a reading culture; the one that matters most is the last, the love of reading those practices are there to grow.

01 · Why this matters

Reading aloud does the most.

Reading for pleasure is linked to both attainment and wellbeing, and a reading culture is built by adults who read aloud, model being readers, and make books visible and valued.

Of all the practices here, regular reading aloud, well and often, is the single most powerful. The book boxes, door signs and stands are the visible scaffolding around it.

Teresa Cremin's research links reading for pleasure to both attainment and wellbeing, and shows that a reading culture is built by adults who read aloud and model being readers themselves.after Teresa Cremin · the DfE Reading Framework
02 · The sequence

The practices, and the point.

The gold beats are reading aloud and the love of reading itself: the first is the most powerful practice, the last is the aim of all of them.

03 · In practice

Building the culture.

Three habits make a reading culture visible and real.

  1. Read aloud to the class regularly and well, as the single most powerful practice in the pillar (RFP1).
  2. Keep books visible and valued: door signs up to date, Hounslow book boxes on the shelves, books displayed on stands (RFP2 to RFP4).
  3. Make the Friday book club and the timetabled library slot count, and watch for pupils choosing to read (RFP5 to RFP7).
04 · Pitfalls

What goes wrong, and the fix.

RFP1Reading aloud squeezed out. It is the practice with the most impact, and the first to be lost when time is short.
RFP7Display without culture. Book boxes and stands matter only insofar as they help pupils actually want to read.
