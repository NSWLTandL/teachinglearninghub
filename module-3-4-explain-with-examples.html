Orientation

Five concepts, one purpose.

This module covers worked examples and the discipline of using both correct and incorrect examples to define a concept's boundary. Anchored to ID8 (check for understanding) and the source booklet's incorrect-examples technique.

01 · Why this matters

The boundary is the concept.

A pupil who has seen a correct example knows what a thing is. A pupil who has seen a correct and an incorrect example knows what the thing is and where it stops being that thing. The boundary is what produces robust understanding; it is what allows pupils to recognise the concept in unfamiliar contexts and to spot it failing in their own work.

Worked examples reduce cognitive load (Rosenshine, Sweller) by giving pupils a complete, expert-produced solution to study before attempting their own. WAGOLL — what a good one looks like — is the standard form. The complement is WABOLL — what a bad one looks like — and is the discipline most teachers use intuitively but plan for less explicitly.

The source booklet captures this in an observed lesson: a teacher recognised a misconception and asked the class "what would the student have done wrong to get the answer X? With a partner, go!" The technique was instinctive but powerful. Planning for bad examples — building them deliberately into the I Do — is the next step.

Step 1 · Worked example

The complete, expert solution.

ID3ID8

The worked example is what the I Do produces — a finished, expert-produced solution that pupils can study before attempting their own. The Australian research from Rosenshine showed that pupils who alternate between studying worked examples and solving similar problems outperform pupils who only solve problems. The example is the scaffold; the practice is the test.

What makes a worked example work as a teaching tool: every step is visible, every step is named, the underlying principle is identified. "Step three — multiply both sides by three. PAUSE Why? Because we are isolating x, and the coefficient is one third." The example shows the trail and the reasoning at the same time.

Scripted worked example — Year 9 algebra"Step one — collect like terms. WRITE · NAME Step two — divide both sides by the coefficient. WRITE · NAME Why? PAUSE · 1 SEC Because we want x on its own."

Step 2 · Wording principle

Identical language, one important word different.

When showing a correct and an incorrect example side by side, the language used to introduce them is identical except for one word. The difference is the polarity — "is" versus "is not", "strong" versus "weak", "correct" versus "incorrect". The pupil's working memory does not have to track changing vocabulary; it can focus on the difference between the two examples.

The source booklet illustrates the principle directly:

Wording principle — strong vs weak topic sentenceUse: "This is a strong topic sentence. PAUSE This is not a strong topic sentence."

Avoid: "This is a strong topic sentence. But this one is a poorly-written example of a paragraph start."

The "avoid" version uses different vocabulary for the two examples ("strong topic sentence" / "poorly-written example of a paragraph start"). The pupil now has to translate between two phrasings before they can compare the two examples. The "use" version puts the cognitive work where it belongs — on the difference between the examples themselves.

Build shared vocabulary across the unit. The phrase used to introduce examples is the phrase pupils will use when discussing them. Identical wording becomes shared vocabulary; varied wording becomes confusion.
3
Step 3 · Juxtaposition

Minimally different examples. Show the boundary.

Juxtaposition is the technique of placing minimally different examples next to each other to define the boundary of a concept. The two examples differ in one feature only; that feature is what distinguishes "is" from "is not". The pupil sees the boundary directly rather than inferring it from a description.

The source booklet's example: a horizontal line drawn through a shape that creates a line of symmetry, paired with a horizontal line drawn through a similar shape that does not. Both shapes are similar; both lines are horizontal; the only difference is whether the line actually divides the shape into two mirror halves. The juxtaposition shows what symmetry is by showing what it isn't, in the form most economical for working memory.

Juxtaposition — line of symmetry"This is a line of symmetry. POINT · PAUSE This is not a line of symmetry. POINT · PAUSE Why is this one not? PAUSE · 3 SEC · COLD-CALL"

Juxtaposition works for: definitions where the boundary is hard to describe in words (symmetry, perpendicular, parallel); concepts where the surface details are similar but the structure differs (strong topic sentence, weak topic sentence); procedures where one step is performed correctly in one example and incorrectly in another (showing where the error happens, not just that it happens).

Visual WABOLL gallery

"This is. This is not."

Three worked examples of visual juxtaposition. In each pair, the only feature that differs is the one that matters. The pupil sees the boundary directly because the surrounding visual context is held constant. The source booklet's line-of-symmetry pairing is the template; the principle generalises to any concept whose boundary is visual.

Line of symmetry
Both shapes identical. Both lines horizontal. The boundary is only whether the line bisects through the axis of symmetry — and the pupil can see it.
Right angle
The slight tilt is the entire point. A WABOLL that is obviously wrong teaches nothing; a WABOLL that looks nearly right teaches the boundary.
Parallel lines
The drift is small enough that a quick glance reads "parallel". Pupils discover that parallel is a precise condition, not a visual impression.
The discipline of the WABOLL is the small difference. A bad example that is wildly wrong shows what a wrong answer looks like; a bad example that is slightly wrong shows where the concept stops being itself. The "slightly wrong" version teaches the boundary; the wildly wrong version teaches nothing.
4
Step 4 · Plan the bad examples

WABOLL designed in advance, not improvised.

ID8

The instinct to use a "bad example" is widespread. The discipline is to plan it. Before the lesson, the teacher writes down the three or four most common ways pupils get this wrong, and uses one of them as the WABOLL — what a bad one looks like.

The teacher in the source booklet asked, "what would the student have done wrong to get the answer X?" That is the planning question. For every concept, what is the most common pupil error? For every procedure, where does it most often go wrong? The answers become the bad examples used during the I Do, paired with the WAGOLL using the wording principle and juxtaposition above.

Planned WABOLLs do two things instinctive ones cannot. They surface specific known misconceptions rather than producing generic wrong answers. And they let the teacher use the same wording across lessons and across the year, so pupils develop a shared vocabulary for talking about errors as well as for talking about correct work.

Plan one WABOLL per lesson. Add it to the lesson plan alongside the WAGOLL. Over a half-term, the bank of WABOLLs becomes a teaching resource — the planned wrong answers that exemplify the procedures most reliably.
Coaching moment · plan a WABOLL10 minutes · individual then pairs
  1. Identify one concept from this week's lessons that pupils typically misunderstand.
  2. Write the WAGOLL — the correct example — with the specific wording you will use to introduce it.
  3. Write the WABOLL — a minimally different incorrect example. The difference is one feature; the wording mirrors the WAGOLL with one word changed.
  4. For the WABOLL, write one sentence on the misconception it exemplifies. This is what you will probe with the class after juxtaposition.
Coach prompt: Is the WABOLL a genuine plausible error a pupil would make, or a nonsense distractor? Genuine errors teach; nonsense distractors do not.
03 · Pitfalls

Where examples teaching breaks.

Each pitfall describes a teacher behaviour that reduces the diagnostic value of examples teaching.

Only correct examples.Risks ID8

The teacher shows three or four worked examples that all do the procedure correctly. The pupil sees what the procedure looks like when right, but not where it most often goes wrong. The boundary is not defined.

WABOLL improvised mid-lesson.Risks ID8

The teacher generates a wrong answer on the spot. It may not match the misconceptions pupils actually hold; it may be a wrong answer no pupil would actually produce. Plan the WABOLL.

Different vocabulary for correct and incorrect.Risks shared vocabulary

"This is a strong topic sentence. This one is poorly written." The pupil now has two phrases to track. Use identical wording with one polarity word changed.

Examples too different to compare.Risks juxtaposition

The correct and incorrect examples differ in many features. The pupil cannot identify which feature is the boundary. Minimally different — one feature changes.

Bad examples shown without analysis.Risks ID8

The teacher shows a wrong answer and says "and this is wrong" without naming why or what misconception it reveals. The pupil sees the wrong answer but does not learn what makes it wrong.

Using bad examples to embarrass pupils.Risks ID8 and culture

"This is what [Pupil] wrote yesterday." The pupil now associates examples teaching with public correction. Bad examples are anonymous and constructed; they are teaching tools, not consequences.
