Starting here

Five sets of techniques, one lesson.

The five sessions so far each took a part of teaching on its own. A real lesson uses all of them at once. This session is about fitting them together across an hour, and — just as importantly — about how a teacher and a school make these things into habits rather than a fortnight's good intentions.

The honest truth about professional development is that most of it fades. A good session is delivered, everyone agrees it was sensible, and three weeks later the classroom looks exactly as it did before. What separates training that sticks from training that doesn't is not the quality of the ideas — it is whether there is deliberate, repeated practice afterwards, with someone giving feedback. This session sets that up, and works two ways: as a map for building the techniques into your own lessons, and as a guide for rolling the programme out across a department or school.

Across one lesson

Where each part belongs.

A single lesson, with the five sessions mapped onto it. Not a script to follow rigidly — a sense of which tool belongs where.

At the door and the start.

Presence and routines do the opening. Meet the class at the door, settle them with your usual entry routine, and start them on something that needs no instruction. The lesson is calm and moving before you've given a single new instruction. (Sessions 1 and 2)

The new material.

Explaining and modelling carry the heart of the lesson. Model it with the thinking spoken aloud, in small steps, on something everyone can see. Then practise the next one together before anyone works alone. (Session 3)

Before and during independent work.

Checking takes over. Confirm they know the task before you release them, then work the set-settle-sweep-stop rhythm — moving round, marking as you go, ready to pull the class back if a common error shows up. (Session 4)

All the way through, to keep them thinking.

Questioning runs across the whole lesson. Ask-pause-name to check the explanation landed, probe answers to get at the reasoning, and use think-pair-share before the harder questions. It's both a teaching tool and a constant check. (Session 5)

The close.

Routines and presence come back for the ending. A last check of the key idea, a clean pack-away on your signal, and the class leaves in order when the room is settled — finishing on your terms. (Sessions 1 and 2)

Making it stick

One technique at a time.

The single most useful thing to know about changing your practice: pick one technique at a time. Trying to change everything at once changes nothing. Choose a single move — say, ask-pause-name — and use it every lesson until it's automatic, then add the next. A teacher who properly masters one technique a fortnight has changed their practice by the end of a term, which is far more than a teacher who tries all five at once and keeps none.

A technique becomes a habit through repetition with feedback, not through agreeing it's a good idea. Decide what you're going to practise, practise it daily, and ask someone to watch for it. That last part — someone watching and telling you honestly — is what most professional development leaves out, and it is the part that works.
Rolling it out

For a department or school.

Run the sessions over a term, not in a day.

One session every two or three weeks, with the gap used to practise that session's techniques. A single training day is forgotten by half-term; a programme spread out has time to embed.

Pair teachers up.

The coaching task in each session works because someone watches and gives specific feedback on one thing. Lasting improvement comes from low-stakes peer observation focused on a single technique, not from one-off appraisal.

Use the same words across the staff.

When everyone names the same moves — "ask-pause-name," "set-settle-sweep-stop" — feedback gets quick and specific. A shared vocabulary is half the value of doing this together rather than alone.

Keep this site as the reference.

After each live session, these pages are where teachers come back to check a technique or re-read the steps and the checklist. The programme doesn't end when the session does.

Coaching task

Plan one whole lesson.

20 minutes · in pairs

All five, in one hour.

  1. Each take a real lesson coming up this week.
  2. Mark on it where each part belongs — the opening, the new material, independent work, questioning throughout, the close.
  3. Choose the one technique you'll most deliberately practise in it, and tell your partner which.
  4. Agree to watch each other teach it — even informally — and feed back on that one thing only.
One lesson · all five · one thing to practise
Last word

Where to go from here.

You now have the whole set: the presence to lead a room, the routines to keep it calm, a clear way to explain and model, the checks to know it landed, and the questioning to keep every pupil thinking — and a way to fit them together and make them stick. Any session is there to come back to from the menu at the top. The techniques are simple to name and take a career to get right, which is rather the point of them.
