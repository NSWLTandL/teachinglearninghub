Orientation

Three jobs, one routine.

Circulation is the deliberate, purposeful movement of the teacher around the room during independent practice. It is not a wander. It has three jobs: to check that pupils are on task, to check whether the work is being done correctly, and to intervene early, before a misconception takes root. This module is the routine that makes those three jobs happen reliably, every lesson.

01 · Why this matters

The teacher at the front is guessing.

The teacher who stays at the front during independent practice cannot see whether the explanation has landed. They are reading the room from the one position in it that reveals the least. The teacher who circulates is reading the room in real time: who has understood, who is faking it, who is stuck, and who has finished and is waiting to be stretched.

Lemov's technique describes circulation built on four ideas. The teacher breaks the plane — moving out from behind the desk into the rows within the first thirty seconds. Full access is required — every desk reachable, bags off the floor, chairs tucked in. The teacher engages while circulating rather than walking past. And the teacher moves systematically — a planned circuit, not a random route, so no pupil is missed.

Live marking is feedback at its most efficient. The pupil is sitting in front of the work; the green pen mark is made where they can see it, question it, and correct the error before it embeds. The teacher does not carry the books home, because the marking has already happened. The EEF's central finding holds the standard here: feedback should focus on the task, the subject, and self-regulation, not on the pupil as a person.

The pupil responds in purple pen, and the purple pen is non-negotiable. It is the visible proof that feedback has been acted on. A book full of green pen and no purple pen is a book where the conversation has been one-sided — feedback given, but never taken up.

02 · The virtue connection

Held to a standard worthy of them.

The booklet sets this unit explicitly against the Nishkam virtues: to align checking and feedback with diligence, self-discipline, and excellence, so that every child is held to a standard worthy of them. Each stage of the cycle carries a virtue, and the three daily classroom virtues run through all of it.

Diligence is the word that opens the routine. "Diligence, thank you" secures attention before the three-step instruction; diligence is also what the teacher is checking for as they sweep — sustained, careful effort, pens moving, eyes on the page. The routine makes diligence observable rather than hoped-for.

Peacefulness is the working environment the circulation protects, and the word that restores it. The booklet's checklist holds that the room is calm and quiet, and is corrected using "peacefulness" if it is not (CCS9). When a widespread error forces a stop, "peacefulness, thank you" is how the room is brought down before the re-teach.

Determination is protected by how the teacher marks. Live marking is a micro-intervention — underline, circle, a word, move on — that hands the correction back to the pupil to make in purple. It does not do the work for them; it points, and then expects the pupil to push through. The standard held in books is the same standard for every pupil, SEND and PP included, with scaffolding in place rather than expectations lowered.

Standards we walk past are the standards we accept. Every page of every book carries a message about what the teacher tolerates. The first time a page is missed, the teacher asks why. The standard is built by what is enforced as the teacher circulates, not by what is written in a policy.

1
Set · the instruction and the check

A clean three-step instruction, then check confidence.

CCS2CCS3CCS4

At the transition into independent work, the teacher gives a clear description of the task as a numbered three-step instruction, counting the steps on fingers. The instruction is tight and free of filler — the test is whether a pupil could repeat it back. The teacher then checks confidence by cold-calling before releasing the class, rather than assuming the instruction has landed.

Scripted set — releasing to independent practice"Diligence, thank you, in 3, 2, 1. Step one — complete questions 1 to 5 in your books. Step two — underline your final answer. Step three — check your work against the model on the board. COLD-CALL Sarah, what is step two? … Correct. When I say go, ten minutes. Independent. In silence. Pens ready. GO"
Check before you release. The cold call after the instruction is the difference between setting the task and assuming it. A pupil who cannot say step two will not do step two.
2
Settle · the first sixty seconds

Hold a strong position. Scan. Be seen looking.

CCS5CCS7

The first sixty seconds matter most. Before moving, the teacher takes a strong position in the room, scans, and is seen looking — reading who has started, who has not, and who is unsure. Praising the first pupil to begin sets the expectation for the rest. The temptation to drift, or to answer the first raised hand, is resisted; the scan comes first.

Scripted settle — the opening scan"Ayesha is already writing. Thank you, Ayesha." SCAN · 30 SEC
Position before movement. Pupils who know the first minute is watched start in the first minute. The strong position, held and visible, does more than any single instruction.
3
Sweep · the planned circuit and live marking

Break the plane. A circuit, not a wander. Mark live; secure the purple pen.

CCS5CCS6CCS8CCS11

At thirty seconds the teacher breaks the plane — moving into the rows — and begins a planned circuit. The path is decided in advance so no pupil is missed; the teacher looks at books, not at the room in general. Where pupils need it, the teacher provides the adaptation or scaffolding that makes the task accessible (CCS6), and where an assistant teacher is present, their support is directed to key pupils (CCS8).

Live marking happens at the desk, in green pen, in the moment. Each intervention is short, specific, and actionable — under ten seconds. The teacher underlines, circles, comments, and moves on; the pupil responds immediately in purple pen before the teacher leaves (CCS11). Teaching from the back of the room is more powerful than the front: from behind, the teacher sees every page and every glance, and pupils cannot tell whether they are being watched.

Scripted sweep — three desks on the circuit(Underlines a word in green: "check spelling.") "Read that back. Fix it." PURPLE PEN … (Circles an answer: "re-read the question.") "Try again. I'll be back." … (Ticks twice: "strong — use this sentence later.") "Excellent. Keep going."
Engage, do not pass. A circuit that does not stop is just walking. Lean in, point at something, make one mark, secure the purple response, move on.
4
Stop · re-teach when the error is widespread

When the same error appears three times, stop the class.

CCS9CCS10

As the teacher sweeps, they evaluate success and error and decide whether to keep correcting individually or to stop the whole class (CCS10). The threshold is a judgement, but the booklet's signal is clear: when the same error appears three times, an individual fix is no longer efficient. The teacher stops, brings the room down with "peacefulness," re-models briefly under the visualiser, and resets the class to correct in purple.

Scripted stop — the whole-class reset"Peacefulness, thank you. Pens down. Eyes this way. Three of you have written X when the answer is Y. Let me show you why." RE-MODEL "Go back to question 3. Correct it in purple. Two minutes. GO"
Stop early, not late. The cost of stopping the class is a minute. The cost of not stopping is twenty pupils embedding the same misconception. The threshold is lower than instinct suggests.
Coaching moment · the first sixty seconds15 minutes · quartets, rotate roles
  1. One teacher, two pupils, one coach. The teacher has just modelled a worked example and must now release the class to independent practice.
  2. The teacher gives a clean three-step instruction, checks confidence with a cold call, holds a strong position for thirty seconds, then breaks the plane.
  3. One pupil starts promptly; the other is slow — pen down, looking around. The teacher responds with proximity, not words.
  4. Coach checks: was the instruction tight and numbered? Was confidence checked before release? Did the teacher hold position before circulating?
Coach prompt: Where did the instruction stop being necessary and start being filler? What signal told you the class was ready to be released?
04 · Quality of work

The book is a record of a year's thinking.

Quality of work is the visible standard of the books the teacher accepts: presentation, volume, care, and a clear narrative of increasing depth across the term. It is not pedantry. A well-kept book signals to a pupil that their work matters, that someone is watching, and that they are capable of more than they think.

The reverse is equally true. The dog-eared, half-empty, scrawled-in book communicates that this is not important, that the teacher does not look, that nothing here is worth keeping. Standards in books do not emerge by accident — they are built by the routines enforced at the start of every lesson, dates and titles underlined, and by the standards enforced on the circuit.

Where presentation has fallen short, the standard is raised publicly but generally, never by naming the pupil. The point is the standard, not the shame. "I picked up two books with no underlined date. That is not the standard. From today, no work is started until the date and title are underlined." The book is corrected; the child is not humiliated.

The same standard for every pupil. Regardless of SEND or PP status, the same volume and quality of work is expected, with appropriate scaffolding in place. Lower expectations, dressed as kindness, communicate that the teacher believes the pupil capable of less.
05 · Pitfalls

Where circulation breaks.

Each pitfall describes a teacher behaviour that reduces the diagnostic value of circulation and checking.

Teaching from the front.Risks CCS5

The teacher stays at the desk during independent practice, protecting themselves rather than their pupils. They cannot see whether the work has landed and are reduced to guessing. Break the plane within thirty seconds.

The wander, not the circuit.Risks CCS5

The teacher moves, but the path is instinctive and uneven. Some pupils are visited three times; others are missed entirely. Plan the route so every desk is reached.

Green pen, no purple pen.Risks CCS11

The teacher marks live but moves on before the pupil responds. The feedback is given but never taken up; the conversation is one-sided. Secure the purple pen response before leaving the desk.

Correcting individually past the threshold.Risks CCS10

The same error appears at desk after desk, and the teacher keeps fixing it one pupil at a time. The efficient move was passed several desks ago. Stop the class and re-teach.

Live marking that is not actionable.Risks CCS11

The teacher writes "good" or a long comment the pupil cannot act on in the moment. Live marking is short, specific, and immediate — underline, name the fix, move on.

Shaming presentation.Risks the climate

"This is what [Pupil]'s book looks like." The standard is raised by naming a child, who now associates book standards with public correction. Raise the standard generally; the books are corrected without naming whose fell short.
