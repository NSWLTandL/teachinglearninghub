Starting here

If they can't follow the explanation, nothing else helps.

This is the centre of the whole programme. You can have a perfectly behaved room and ask good questions, but if the explanation itself isn't clear, pupils learn the wrong thing or nothing at all. Explaining and modelling well is the most useful thing a teacher does, and it is the part most worth getting right. This session is the model we use — broken into the same three stages, with the same things we look for — generalised so it fits any subject and any school.

The idea underneath it is simple: a pupil can only hold so much at once, and every extra word, tangent, or unclear step uses up room they need for the actual learning. So we teach new material in small steps, show each one properly, and let pupils try it before moving on. The shorthand for the three stages is "I do, we do, you do" — you model it, the class practises it with you, then they do it on their own. Each stage has its own steps, and they are below.

The first stage

I do — modelling it.

You show how it is done, and just as importantly, how you think while doing it. The aim is for pupils to see the thinking, not just the finished answer.

Say why it matters first.

Before you model, take a moment to say what the thing is for — what pupils will be able to do, and why it is worth knowing. A model that arrives with no purpose is harder to attend to than one a pupil understands the point of.

Think out loud as you go.

Narrate the thinking, not just the steps. Say what you notice, what you're deciding, why you're choosing this over that. Pupils can copy a thought process they can hear; they can't copy one that stays in your head. This is the difference between showing the destination and showing the route.

Model each step, one at a time.

Work through it step by step, talking through each action as you do it. Don't collapse three steps into one because they feel obvious to you — they aren't obvious to someone meeting it for the first time. The small steps feel slow to you and clear to them.

Make sure everyone can see it.

Use a visualiser, board, or flipchart so every pupil can actually see what you're doing. Let it carry its share — point to it rather than describing in words what the picture already shows, or the two compete for attention.

Stand back and check it.

When the model is done, step back and review it with the class — is it right, does it do what was asked? Then check they have followed before you move on, and model it a second way if they haven't. A model nobody understood is not a model; it's a performance.

The second stage

We do — practising it together.

Between your model and pupils working alone sits guided practice. You work the next one with the class, prompting the steps rather than supplying them. It is the bridge that catches misunderstandings while they are still easy to fix.

Say clearly that you're moving on.

Mark the move into "we do" out loud, so pupils know the job has changed from watching to joining in. A clear verbal handover stops the stage from blurring into more modelling.

Use an example that matches your model.

The one you practise together should be close to the one you just modelled — near enough that the model is obviously useful, not so different that pupils are starting from scratch again.

Guide them through it, pointing back to the model.

Prompt the steps rather than doing them — "what's the first move?" — and keep referring back to the modelled example so pupils learn to lean on it. Done together, out loud, this is where the class rehearses the thinking with your support still there. Where it fits, use choral response — the whole class answering together on a cue — to keep everyone involved and to hear, in one go, who is with you and who isn't.

Check they're ready before letting go.

Before independent work, check whether the class is actually ready for it. This is the same checking you'll meet in Session 4 — a quick look at whether they can do it with less of you, before you remove yourself entirely.

The third stage

You do — independent practice.

Pupils now do it on their own. Your job shifts to watching closely and keeping them anchored to the model.

Mark the move to working alone.

Say clearly that pupils are now working independently, so the change of expectation is unmistakable — this is their turn, not a continuation of the shared practice.

Keep them using the model.

As they work, check the modelled example is actually being used rather than ignored. The model is there to be leaned on; part of your circulation is making sure it is.

Keep checking as they go.

Independent practice is not a chance to switch off — it is where you find out whether the teaching landed. Circulate and check, ready to pull the class back together if a common problem shows up. That checking is the whole of Session 4.

Saying less

Fewer words, more learning.

Running through all three stages, one habit matters more than any single technique: saying less. Every extra word spends a bit of a pupil's attention. Use plain, consistent phrasing, give an instruction once, and let a pause do the work a repetition would. This isn't about being cold — it's about respecting how little a pupil can hold at once, and spending it on the idea rather than on your talk around it.

A useful test: write out an explanation you give often, then cut it by a third and read both aloud. The shorter one almost always lands better. The words you cut were nearly always doing work for you, not for them.
Where it tends to go wrong

Three things to watch.

Setting work you never modelled.

Sending pupils into a task you didn't demonstrate leaves them to work out a method on their own, often the wrong one. Model it first, thinking aloud.

Talking about it instead of showing it.

Describing, at length, how one might approach a problem, without ever performing the move. Pupils hear a description but see nothing to copy. Say the step, do it, say why, move on.

Moving on because the room is quiet.

Quiet isn't the same as understanding. Without a check you're guessing — so build the check in before you press ahead.

The checklist

What a colleague would look for.

This is the self-audit we use for explaining and modelling, set out one-to-one with our own checklist, with the wording generalised. It runs in the same three stages as the lesson. Rate each red, amber, or green.

I do — modelling

ID1 · Talks through the purpose of the model.

What is the objective, why this matters, and how it links to the wider learning.

ID2 · Narrates the thinking through the problem or task.

What is being asked, what we already know, and what we are aiming for.

ID3 · Models each stage step by step, talking through each action.

Nothing is collapsed or skipped; every step is shown and explained.

ID4 · Uses a suitable platform so all pupils can see — visualiser, board, or flipchart.

Every pupil can clearly see the model being built.

ID5 · Pupils are actively listening, not doing anything else.

At the modelling stage, attention is on the model and nothing else.

ID6 · Narrates what to do next, and why.

The reasoning behind each next step is made explicit, not just the step.

ID7 · Stands back to review the model and evaluate it.

You step back and judge whether it is correct and meets the success criteria.

ID8 · Checks for understanding and models alternatives as needed.

Understanding is confirmed, and a second way is modelled if it hasn't landed.

We do — guided practice

WD1 · Makes the move to 'we do' clear out loud.

The shift from watching to joining in is signalled explicitly.

WD2 · Uses a task matched to the modelled example.

The shared practice is close enough to the model to make it obviously useful.

WD3 · Guides practice with reference to the model — including choral response where it fits.

You prompt the steps and point back to the model, using whole-class response where it helps.

WD4 · Checks for understanding before independent practice.

You confirm the class is ready before they work alone.

You do — independent practice

YD1 · Makes the move to 'you do' clear out loud.

The shift to working independently is signalled clearly.

YD2 · Moves pupils towards working independently.

Pupils take over the task on their own.

YD3 · Checks the modelled example is being used.

The model is leaned on, not ignored, as pupils work.

YD4 · Checks for understanding.

You keep checking that the learning is landing as they work alone.

Coaching task

Sharpen one explanation.

20 minutes · in pairs

Model it, cut it, check it.

  1. Each choose one thing you'll teach this week that pupils often find hard.
  2. Script the "I do" as small steps with the thinking spoken aloud, then plan the matched "we do" example.
  3. Read it to your partner. They cut any words that just repeat the visual or pad a step.
  4. Agree the one check you'll use to know it landed before you let pupils work alone.
