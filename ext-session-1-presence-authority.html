Presence & Authority.

Before any technique works, a class has to be willing to be led. This is the routine we use to settle that — the five-step instruction, the start at the door, and what a colleague would look for — with our own school's wording taken out so you can put your own in.

Starting here

A class reads you before it hears you.

Everything in the sessions that follow depends on a class being willing to be led, and that willingness is settled in the first minute of a lesson — before you have given a single instruction — by how you carry yourself. This session is the routine we use to establish it. It is the same sequence, the same words, and the same things we look for in a lesson; we have only taken out the parts that are specific to our school, so you can put your own in.

Presence has very little to do with personality, and nothing to do with volume. It is a small number of things you can see a teacher doing — where they stand, how still they are, where they look, how they use their voice — and because they are observable, they can be practised until they are second nature. The rest of this page takes them one at a time, gives you the words to use, and shows you how to rehearse each one.

The instruction routine

Five steps for giving an instruction.

This is the core of it, and it is the same every time you ask a class to do something. Five steps, in order. Once the sequence is automatic, most low-level non-compliance simply falls away, because the instruction is clear, the pause is held, and you have made it plain that you expect it to be followed.

Get their attention before you ask.

Secure attention first. Take a settled position at the front, use your attention signal, and wait until you actually have the room before you give the instruction. An instruction given over a half-listening class has to be given again; one given into a settled room only has to be given once.

The words: "[Attention cue], thank you — in 3, 2, 1." Put your own settle cue in place of the bracket — whatever your school already uses works fine.

Keep the instruction to five words or fewer.

Short, plain, and without the padding. "Books open, page forty-seven" — not "so what I'd like everyone to do now, if you wouldn't mind, is turn to page forty-seven." The long version invites a negotiation and hides the instruction inside it. The short version leaves no room for doubt about what you have asked.

The words: "Books open. Page forty-seven." / "Pens down. Eyes this way." Use the fewest words that still carry the meaning.

Wait, and let them do it.

Once you have given the instruction, stop talking. Scan the room and give the class the few seconds they need to act on it. The silence will feel longer to you than it does to them, and it matters. If you fill it — repeating, adding "come on now, please, everyone" — you teach the class that the instruction was not really meant the first time.

What to do: Give the instruction, then count three seconds to yourself while you scan the room. Say nothing until you have looked across the whole class.

Acknowledge it quietly, and move on.

When the class does what you asked, there is no need to thank them warmly or make a point of it. A small nod, and on with the lesson. Making a fuss of ordinary compliance quietly suggests that following an instruction is something out of the ordinary, when the whole point is that it is simply what happens here.

What to do: A single nod, then straight into the next part of the lesson. No running commentary.

If it doesn't happen, ask once more — calmly.

If a pupil hasn't done what you asked, give the same instruction again, once, in the same words and the same even tone — not louder, not sharper. If it still doesn't happen, move to a calm, named consequence. You are working through a system everyone knows, not having an argument. Your tone does not change, and that steadiness is most of what keeps your authority intact.

The words: Same instruction, same tone — "Books open. Page forty-seven." Then, if you need it: "[Name], that's a [your first consequence]. Now — books open."
The start of the lesson

It begins at the door.

The best chance you get to establish your presence is right at the start, at the door. Stand where pupils pass as they come in — where they can see you, settled and ready — and set the expectation for entry as they arrive. They walk past a teacher who is prepared, with a task already waiting that needs no further explanation. The teacher who is still sorting out the laptop as pupils come in gives that moment away.

There is one small habit here worth singling out, because it does a lot of work and carries over to any classroom unchanged. When you set a class going, telling them "when I say go, go" — and then holding a deliberate pause before "go" — teaches them that nothing starts until you say so. It sounds almost too simple to matter, and it matters a great deal.

So: set the task before you release them. State it in three steps or fewer, check that one pupil can say it back to you, and only then release them with the "go." The start of the lesson is then calm, already moving, and on your terms rather than the class's.

The words: "Here's the task — three steps. One, [...]. Two, [...]. Three, [...]. [Name], what's step two? … Good. When I say go, go. (pause) … Go."
Practising it

Rehearse the routine before you rely on it.

The five-step routine only works when it is automatic, and it only becomes automatic if you practise it. It is worth drilling the steps on their own, away from a live class, before you lean on them in a lesson.

Practise giving the instruction and then waiting.

On your own, or with a colleague, practise giving a five-word instruction and then holding a silent three-second scan. Most teachers find the waiting the hardest part — the instinct is to rush past it. Rehearse it until the silence feels ordinary rather than uncomfortable.

Practise asking again without your voice rising.

Practise giving the same instruction a second time in exactly the same even tone, with no rise in volume and no edge. Have a colleague play a little reluctance; your job is to keep your tone exactly the same. This is the step that keeps you composed when a class tests you, which it will.

The checklist

What a colleague would look for.

These are the things someone watching your lesson would look for. It is the same self-audit we use, with the items that name our own school taken out. Rate yourself against each, or ask a colleague to — red, amber, or green. The checklist is the method; the steps above are how you meet it.

P1 · The room is calm and purposeful when someone walks in.

At any moment, an observer arriving sees a settled, focused room. The climate is set deliberately, not left to chance.

P2 · Pupils are focused on the task — either listening or working.

At any point, each pupil is doing the one thing the lesson asks of them right now.

P3 · The teacher pauses for pupils to comply, and scans for full attention.

After an instruction there is a held pause and a look across the whole room, rather than pressing straight on.

P4 · Instructions are clear, and no more than three steps.

Every instruction is short enough to hold in your head and act on the first time.

P5 · Pupils follow an instruction first time.

The class acts on the first asking, because the routine and the pause leave no doubt about what is expected.

P6 · Behaviour is handled through where you stand and a calm reset.

Small issues are dealt with by position and proximity and a quiet reset of the expectation — not by raising your voice.

P7 · The teacher is at the door as the lesson begins.

Presence is set at the door, with the expectation for coming in made clear as pupils arrive.

P8 · Eye contact and scanning keep pupils on task.

A steady, unhurried look around the room, used throughout, lets the class know they are seen.

Coaching task

Practise it with a colleague.

15 minutes · in pairs

The five-step instruction.

  1. One of you takes the teacher's part; the other watches against the five steps of the routine.
  2. The teacher runs through it: get attention, give the instruction in five words, wait and scan, acknowledge with a nod, ask once more if needed.
  3. The observer comments on one step only — usually the wait, since that is the one most often skipped.
  4. Swap over, then each run it once more, fixing the step the observer pointed to. Put your own attention cue and consequence into the script.
