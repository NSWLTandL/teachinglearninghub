Session 2 · Self-study

Leading from the middle

You translate the school’s vision into the daily work of a team. This area sets out the six behaviours of effective middle leadership, and how consistency turns them into momentum.

“Ambiguity causes drift. Clarity creates momentum.”

The work

Turning vision into action

You are the translator of vision into action. Leading from the middle is six behaviours, practised day in, day out, until they become how your team works.

1 · Clarity of purpose

You translate the school’s vision into what good looks like here.

In practice

  • Set clear expectations for what good looks like.
  • Communicate non-negotiables in a way that invites ownership.
  • Align meetings, CPD, data and conversations.
  • Why it matters: ambiguity causes drift; clarity creates momentum.

2 · Visible, approachable presence

Leadership is seen before it is heard.

In practice

  • Walk the corridors; drop into lessons; be there at key moments.
  • Notice effort; praise publicly and personally.
  • Be available for informal check-ins.
  • Why it matters: people respond to leaders they trust and see.

3 · Consistency in follow-through

You model what you mean.

In practice

  • Do what you say you will — every time.
  • Apply policies fairly and transparently.
  • Don’t shy away from difficult conversations.
  • Why it matters: inconsistency undermines your integrity and authority.

4 · Developing others

Your success is measured by the growth of your team.

In practice

  • Coach rather than solve: ‘what do you think would work?’
  • Delegate meaningfully; share responsibility.
  • Spot potential and create stretch opportunities.
  • Why it matters: empowered staff are more engaged and effective.

5 · Accountability with compassion

Accountability is not aggression — it is clarity with care.

In practice

  • Use data and evidence to support a conversation, not as a weapon.
  • Frame it around shared values and pupil outcomes.
  • Follow up — consistently and professionally.
  • Why it matters: without accountability culture slips; without compassion morale does.

6 · Reflective, adaptive practice

You grow so others can too.

In practice

  • Seek feedback from your team and peers.
  • Reflect on what worked and what didn’t — and tweak.
  • Model learning, humility, and a little vulnerability.
  • Why it matters: no one follows a leader who believes they have arrived.
Two ideas to hold onto

Culture is built in the small moments

What you permit, you promote (Doug Lemov) — culture is shaped not by what we say but by what we allow. Walk past a missed routine or a quiet drop in standards, and you signal it is acceptable. Silence is not neutral; what we tolerate, we teach.
Consistency is the platform (Paul Bambrick-Santoyo) — consistency is not the enemy of creativity — it is the platform that lets it flourish. Clear routines and feedback free teachers to focus on great teaching rather than guessing at shifting priorities.

Leading from the middle is not enforcement; it is reinforcing the behaviours and values you want to see — by following up, coaching in the moment, and staying visible in holding the line.

The Nishkam frame

Clarity with care

Holding the line and holding people up are the same act, done well.

Service first. You hold standards because you are working for pupils and staff, not for control.
Consistency as kindness. Predictability is what makes a team feel safe enough to take risks.
Optimism. You name the gap and the way back to standard in the same breath.
Follow-through. The quiet, repeated choice to do what you said is what builds trust.

  3. What is one routine or expectation you could make more consistent this week?
