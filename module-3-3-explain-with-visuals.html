Explain with Visuals.

Orientation

Five concepts, one purpose.

This module covers visuals — pairing the spoken word with what pupils see on the shared surface. Anchored to ID4 (visualiser) and ID7 (stand back to review).

01 · Why this matters

Two channels, one message.

Working memory has two channels — verbal and visual — and each can hold three or four items independently. A model delivered through both channels at once doubles the working-memory capacity available to the pupil. Speech alone uses one channel; speech paired with a written or drawn representation uses two.

This is the dual-coding principle from Paivio and Mayer. The pairing is the technique: a diagram with no narration, or a narration with no diagram, uses one channel. A diagram drawn under the visualiser while the teacher narrates each line uses both. The pupil encodes the procedure twice — once aurally, once visually — and the two encodings reinforce each other in long-term memory.

The two ID items below are the structural standards for visual modelling at Nishkam School. ID4 names the surface — visualiser, IWB, or flipchart, visible to all pupils. ID7 names the moment of consolidation — the teacher stepping back from the completed model to review it as a finished object before the We Do begins.

1
Step 1 · Shared surface

Visualiser, IWB, or flipchart. Visible to all.

ID4

The visualiser is the standard surface at Nishkam School. The teacher writes or draws on paper under the visualiser; the image is projected; every pupil sees what is being produced as it is being produced. The IWB and the flipchart are alternatives where the visualiser is not available; the principle is the same — every pupil sees the model emerge.

What the visualiser produces that a slide does not: the procedure as it unfolds. A pre-prepared slide shows the finished result; the visualiser shows the thinking that produced the result. The pupil watches the procedure happen, which is what the I Do is for. A finished worked example presented as a slide is a worked example to copy from, not a model to learn from.

The visualiser sits in the centre of the room's attention. Pre-position it before the lesson. Confirm the projection is sharp and the writing surface is clear of yesterday's residue. The seconds spent on this before pupils arrive are seconds saved during the model.
2
Step 2 · Dual coding

Words and visuals, simultaneous.

ID4ID3

The teacher writes or draws while narrating. The two channels are synchronised: the word "step one" is said as the words "step one" are written; the diagram of the cell is drawn as the parts are named; the equation is written as the operations are spoken. The pupil sees the visual and hears the word at the same instant.

What works as a visual: flow charts that show the order of steps; diagrams that show the structure of a thing; symbols that recur across the unit and acquire shared meaning with the class; arrows that show direction or causation; brackets that show grouping. What does not work as a visual: decoration that is not load-bearing in the explanation. Anything that is on the visualiser is part of the explanation; anything not part of the explanation does not belong on the visualiser.

Scripted dual coding — Year 8 cell biology"Step one — draw the cell membrane." DRAW · NAME "Inside the membrane — the cytoplasm." SHADE · NAME "Inside the cytoplasm — the nucleus, here." DRAW · NAME · POINT
3
Step 3 · Stand back

Step back. Review against the criteria.

ID7

When the model is complete, the teacher physically steps back from the visualiser, pauses, and reviews the finished example aloud. "Right — let's look at what we've got. Step one — cell membrane, drawn. Step two — cytoplasm, shaded. Step three — nucleus, labelled. Does this meet our success criteria?" The model is now treated as an artefact, not as a process.

The standing-back is physical. The teacher actually moves their body away from the visualiser, makes the gesture of evaluating the work as a finished thing. Pupils mirror this — they too are now looking at the model as a completed example to refer to, not as a process they are following step-by-step. The transition is what allows the We Do to begin.

Scripted stand-back"Right — let's look at what we've got. STEP BACK · 2 SEC Step one — drawn. Step two — shaded. Step three — labelled. PAUSE Does this meet our success criteria?" CHORAL · 3-2-1
Coaching moment · run a visual model5 minutes · in pairs
  1. One colleague picks a procedure from this week that has a visual representation (a diagram, a flow chart, a worked example with notation).
  2. Run the three steps: surface set up, dual coding through the steps, physical stand-back at the end.
  3. Partner reports: was every step verbalised as it was drawn? Was the stand-back actually a step back, or just a verbal "let's review"?
Coach prompt: Did the teacher's eyes leave the visualiser to scan the room during the drawing, or were they fixed on the page? The same scanning discipline carries through into the visual model.
03 · Visual restraint

Less, not more.

The temptation with visuals is to add — colour, animation, decorative imagery, supporting graphics. The principle is the opposite. Every visual element on the shared surface competes with every other for the pupil's working memory. Anything that is not load-bearing in the explanation is taking working-memory slots from things that are.

Is there a moving gif of a dancing frog on your power point slide about Pythagoras? It's better to remove it.NSWL Spring TEP Booklet

The dancing-frog gif is the canonical example. The pupil's eye is drawn to the moving image; working memory allocates a slot to processing the movement; the slot is no longer available for processing the actual mathematical content. The visual that is not part of the model is a tax on the model.

The same principle of minimising visual clutter extends into the slide deck. A clean visualiser surface and a clean classroom wall are the same standard applied at different scales. Both protect the same working memory.

Test for any visual: does removing it reduce understanding? If yes, keep it. If no, remove it. The default is removal.
04 · Pitfalls

Where visual modelling breaks.

Each pitfall is tagged with the checklist item it puts at risk.

Pre-prepared slide instead of visualiser.Risks ID4

The finished worked example is shown as a slide; the procedure is not seen happening. Pupils have an example to copy but not a process to learn. The visualiser shows the thinking; the slide shows the trail.

Drawing without narrating.Risks ID3, ID4

The teacher draws the diagram in silence, then explains it after. Dual coding is broken — the verbal channel arrives after the visual, not with it. The two encodings do not reinforce each other.

Narrating without drawing.Risks ID4

The teacher describes a procedure while pupils stare at a blank visualiser. The visual channel is unused; the working memory has only one channel doing the work that two should share.

No stand-back.Risks ID7

The teacher finishes the last step and moves immediately to the We Do. Pupils have not had the moment to register the model as a complete object. The We Do begins without a reference document established.
