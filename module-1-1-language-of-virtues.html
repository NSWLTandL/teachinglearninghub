The Language of Virtues.

01 · Why this matters

The words we choose become the culture they create.

A school that names virtues, day in and day out, builds a different kind of classroom from one that simply asks pupils to "behave."

When a teacher says "stop talking", the pupil hears an instruction. When the teacher says "show me peacefulness", the pupil hears who they are being asked to be. The first ends a behaviour. The second names an identity worth growing into.

This is the heart of the [School] approach. We do not manage behaviour through a transactional vocabulary of warnings, demands and consequences. We build it through a shared, repeated, virtues-based language that pupils encounter from the first lesson of Year 7 to their last assembly in Year 13. The words become predictable. The expectations become internal. And — over time — the pupil who has been named as diligent a thousand times begins, quietly, to believe it.

To empower children with virtues that enable them to excel academically and spiritually, inspiring them to serve humanity selflessly — with an abundance of love, compassion and forgiveness. Mission Statement

The Language of Virtues is not a script we deliver while teaching — it is the medium through which everything else is taught. Every routine in this Pillar (the Diligence mantra, the 30-Second Reflection, the lesson start, the warning ladder) presupposes that the teacher and the pupil share this vocabulary. Get this module right, and the rest of the Pillar lands. Skip it, and the routines feel imposed rather than inhabited.

02 · The research

Why naming the virtue works.

This is not soft pedagogy. The case for virtue-language is supported by behavioural research, cognitive psychology, and decades of classroom observation.

Identity-based framing changes behaviour

Research into identity-based motivation (Oyserman, 2015) shows that children behave in ways consistent with the identities they are repeatedly assigned. Telling a pupil they are diligent is more durable than telling them to be diligent. This is why we say "thank you for showing peacefulness" rather than "thank you for being quiet."

Predictability reduces cognitive load

A consistent vocabulary removes the moment-to-moment guesswork about what the teacher wants. Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory (1988) tells us that every novel signal a pupil has to decode steals capacity from learning. When "diligence" always means the same thing, in every classroom, from every adult, the pupil's working memory is freed for the lesson.

Warm-strict language preserves the relationship

Doug Lemov's Teach Like a Champion describes strict warmth as the most powerful posture a teacher can adopt. Virtues language enables this naturally: "I am asking for diligence because I respect you enough to expect it" is qualitatively different from "stop messing about." The first says I see who you can become. The second says I am annoyed.

Tom Bennett's behavioural toolkit

Bennett's work for the DfE on behaviour in schools repeatedly returns to the same finding: consistency of language across all adults is one of the strongest predictors of school-wide behaviour. A virtues vocabulary, used by every teacher, every TA, every leader, every assembly, is precisely the kind of consistency he describes.

03 · The named virtues

The vocabulary we share.

These are the virtues we name aloud, daily. They function as a public moral grammar. Each has a definition pupils have heard since induction.

The three classroom virtues

These three are spoken constantly. They appear in the lesson-start mantra, in praise, in correction, and in reflection.

  • Peacefulness

    An inner sense of calm. The capacity to be still, to listen, to wait. Peacefulness in the body, peacefulness in the room.

  • Diligence

    Sustained, careful effort. Pens down, arms folded, eyes on the teacher when asked. Pens moving, eyes on the page, when working. The opposite of distraction.

  • Determination

    The will to keep going when work is hard. The pupil who tries again is showing determination. The pupil who gives up is being given the chance to grow into it.

The wider virtues

Used in pastoral conversation, restorative justice, assemblies, and reports. The Pillar makes use of these as needed — when a particular virtue (or its absence) is the precise word for the moment.

  • Respect — for adults, peers, the building, the work itself.
  • Honesty — naming what happened without embellishment or excuse.
  • Kindness — small acts that make others' lives easier.
  • Compassion — feeling for someone, then acting on that feeling.
  • Forgiveness — releasing a grievance after restoration.
  • Trust — the foundation of every productive relationship.
  • Service — using one's gifts for others.

The vices we name

For the language to mean anything, we must also be willing to name what virtues are not. The virtue is paired with a vice, and pupils know both.

Virtue

Peacefulness · Diligence · Determination · Respect · Honesty

Vice (named when needed)

Disturbance · Idleness · Resignation · Disrespect · Dishonesty

We do not avoid these words. We use them rarely, and precisely. "That was not honest, and I think you know it" lands more cleanly than "you lied."

04 · The [School] way

How to use virtues language in a lesson.

There are four moments in every lesson where virtues language is your primary tool: praise, correction, reflection, and reset. Each has a scripted approach. Practise these until they are second nature.

  1. Open with virtues

    Within the first minute of the lesson, the words peacefulness, diligence, or determination should leave your mouth. Most often as part of the lesson-start mantra. The pupils should hear a virtue named before they hear an instruction.

    "Peacefulness, diligence, and determination in 5, 4, 3, 2, 1."

  2. Praise by naming

    When a pupil does the right thing, name the virtue they showed — not the behaviour. Behaviour praise teaches pupils what you want today. Virtue praise teaches them who they are becoming.

    Behaviour praise

    "Good, you got started quickly."

    Virtue praise

    "Positive for [Pupil] — that's diligence the moment you walked in."

  3. Correct by asking

    When a pupil falls short, do not narrate the failure. Ask the question that invites them back. The virtue does the corrective work. Your tone stays warm.

    "[Pupil], are you currently showing me the virtue of diligence?"

    Almost every pupil answers honestly. The few who argue are answered with: "Not to the level we ask for here." — said quietly, and without debate.

  4. Reflect with silence

    The 30-Second Reflection at the start of every lesson asks pupils to consider a single virtue and where they might live it that day. The teacher names the virtue; the room goes silent; pupils sit with it. (Module 1.4 covers this routine in full.)

    "Heads bowed, pens down. Today, the virtue is determination. Where will you need it most this lesson?"

  5. Reset by returning

    When a class drifts mid-lesson, do not raise your voice. Return to your strong position, name the virtue you want, and wait.

    "Let's bring back peacefulness. Pens down, please. Thank you."

    The reset works because the language is shared. You are not introducing a rule — you are calling the room back to a standard everyone already knows.

  6. Close with a thank-you

    End the lesson by naming what the class showed. This is the last impression they leave the room with — and the first one they bring to the next lesson.

    "Thank you, [Class]. That was a lesson of real determination. I'll see you tomorrow."

Annotated transcript — first 90 seconds of a lesson

Teacher (at door): "Morning. Title's on the board. Straight in." — No virtue named yet; that comes once the room is settled.

Teacher (after 50% in): "You have ten seconds to be ready for learning. Thank you, [Pupil] — already working." — Praise targets a specific pupil to anchor the standard.

Teacher (at the lectern): "Peacefulness, diligence, determination in 5, 4, 3, 2, 1." — The mantra. Same words, every lesson, every classroom.

Teacher (head bowed): "Time for reflection. Today's virtue is determination. Where will you need it?" — 30 seconds. No exceptions.

Teacher (after 30 seconds): "Eyes this way." — No second mantra. The first one is doing the work.

06 · Common pitfalls

Where the language breaks down.

The vocabulary only works if it is used precisely. These are the misuses we see most often, and how to avoid them.

Using virtue-words as a substitute for control.

Saying "peacefulness" to a chaotic room while standing at your computer will not produce peacefulness. The word is not a spell. The routine — strong position, scan, pause, reset — produces the calm. The word names it.

Overusing the language.

If every sentence contains a virtue, the words go stale. Use them at the high-leverage moments: opening, praise, correction, reflection, reset, close. In between, talk like a normal teacher.

Naming the vice loudly and the virtue quietly.

If pupils only hear the virtue at the start of the year and the vice every Monday, the framing tilts negative. Praise should outweigh correction — and praise must name a virtue to count.

Staff inconsistency.

The single biggest threat to this system is not the pupils — it is colleagues using "peacefulness" in one room and "shut up" in the next. Coach colleagues into the language. Refer them back to this module if needed. The Pillar collapses without consistency.

Sarcasm.

"Oh, very diligent of you" said witheringly destroys the language for everyone in the room. The vocabulary only works if it is used sincerely. If you cannot say a virtue without irony, do not say it.

07 · Coaching this routine

How to coach a colleague through virtues language.

A coaching quartet protocol. Use in pairs or fours. 15 minutes per round. Rotate roles.

Roles

TeacherDelivers a 90-second lesson opening using the full virtues script.
Pupils (×2)One compliant, one mildly off-task (slow to settle, fidgeting).
CoachObserves silently and uses the questions below.

Coaching questions (in order)

  1. Did you name a virtue within the first 60 seconds?
  2. Was your praise targeted at a specific pupil and a specific virtue?
  3. When you corrected, did you ask a question rather than narrate the problem?
  4. Did the 30-second reflection actually last 30 seconds?
  5. What was your tone? Warm? Strict? Both?
  6. If a colleague were watching, would they have heard the [School] vocabulary, or generic teacher-talk?

Reflection prompt for the teacher

Write three sentences:

  1. One thing you said that landed.
  2. One word you used that you'd swap next time.
  3. One moment you fell back into generic talk.

09 · AI scenario tester

Try a real classroom moment.

Describe a behaviour scenario in your own words. The tool will rephrase your likely response in virtues language and explain why each change matters. Below is a worked example.

Worked example

Scenario tester

The full live tool will accept any classroom scenario you describe. For now, here is one worked example so you can see the shape of what the tool produces.

Teacher submits
"It's minute 8 of the lesson. Three pupils are still chatting at the back. I've already said 'be quiet' twice and they've stopped, then started again. I'm losing the rest of the class. I want to say something firm but I don't want to escalate to a warning yet."
Tool responds

What you said: "Be quiet" — twice. The repetition is the problem. A second "be quiet" tells the room that the first one was optional.

What to say instead, in this order:

  1. Return to your strong position. Stop talking. Wait.
  2. "Peacefulness, please. Pens down, eyes this way." (Said calmly, once.)
  3. Pause. Scan. Be seen looking. Do not continue until 100% compliance.
  4. To the three pupils, quietly: "Are we showing the virtue of peacefulness right now?"
  5. Praise the rest of the class for the diligence they are showing.

Why this works: "Be quiet" is a behavioural demand without a standard attached. "Show peacefulness" names the standard the pupils have heard since Year 7. The question form ("are we showing...") gives them the chance to correct themselves rather than being corrected by you — which preserves the relationship. Praising the compliant majority weights the room's social pressure on your side.

If they still don't comply: You're now at the warning stage. See Module 1.8 — Direct Intervention.
