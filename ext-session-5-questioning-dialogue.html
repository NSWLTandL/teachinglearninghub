Questioning & Dialogue.

Starting here

Questions are for getting everyone thinking.

In a lot of classrooms questioning works like this: the teacher asks, the same three hands go up, one pupil answers, and the rest quietly opt out. The question did its job for one child. Good questioning gets every pupil thinking, every time, because they all know they might be the one to answer. This session is the approach we use — cold calling, probing, and think-pair-share — with the parts that name our school taken out.

It is a small change with a big effect. When pupils know that hands aren't needed and anyone might be asked, they all have to have an answer ready in their heads. The questioning then doubles as checking — you hear real thinking from across the room, not a performance by the confident few. None of this is about catching pupils out; it's about quietly raising the expectation that everyone thinks. The three techniques below each have their own steps.

The first technique

Cold calling — ask, then choose.

You ask the question first and decide who answers afterwards, so everyone has to think. The steps keep it warm and supportive rather than a trap.

Stand where the room can see you, and ask the whole class.

Take a strong position and put a clear, open question to everyone — without naming anyone yet. The question should have a precise answer in mind, so you know what you're listening for.

Give them time to think.

Leave a real pause — a few seconds, judged to the question — and let pupils think before anyone answers. Scan the room while you wait. The silence feels long to you and is exactly what they need; rushing it means only the fastest answer, not the best.

Then name someone.

Only now choose a pupil, by name, to answer. Because the name comes after the question and the pause, everyone has already had to think — naming first lets the rest of the room switch off before the question lands.

Keep the rest listening, and respond to the answer.

The others should be attentive to the answer, not relaxing because it wasn't them. Acknowledge what's given, then either affirm it or probe further. Spread your questions around the class — including the quiet and unsure — warmly, so everyone stays in it.

Use the answers to spot and fix misconceptions.

Listen for what the answers tell you, name and address any misconception that surfaces, and adapt what you do next based on what you hear. The questioning is also a check, so act on it.

The second technique

Probing — following the answer.

Don't stop at a correct answer; follow it. Probing turns a right answer into visible reasoning, and tells the class you want thinking, not just the word.

Open with a question worth thinking about.

Ask an open question that invites pupils to think hard about the key idea, rather than recall a fact. Then choose someone to answer after some think time, as in cold calling.

Take the answer, then ask again.

Acknowledge the contribution, pick out the key idea or rephrase it for clarity, then ask a follow-up that pushes further — clarify, justify, give an example, use the evidence. "Why?" and "how do you know?" are the everyday versions.

Bring the rest of the class in.

Turn the follow-up back to the whole room — "what did you take from that answer?", "do you agree?" — so the probing isn't a private exchange with one pupil. Keep going until the thinking you were after has surfaced.

The third technique

Think, pair, share — rehearsing first.

For a harder question, give pupils a moment to think and then talk it through with a partner before you take answers. When you do ask, even hesitant pupils have something formed to say.

Ask the question, then give think time.

Put a clear, open question to the whole class, then signal think time with a clear timeframe and wait. Pupils think on their own first, before any talking.

Let them talk in pairs.

Ask pupils to discuss in pairs, with a clear time limit and a reminder of what they're aiming for. As they talk, circulate and listen — noting errors and misconceptions, and keeping them on task.

Bring them back and cold-call.

Bring the class back to attention, then cold-call one pupil to give a clear summary of their thinking. Because they've rehearsed it with a partner, the answer is usually fuller and more pupils are willing to give one. Move into probing or further cold calling from there.

Underneath all three

Make it safe to be wrong.

None of this works if pupils are afraid of getting it wrong. The whole point of asking everyone falls apart if a wrong answer is met with a sigh. Treat errors as useful — "interesting, talk me through it" — and build on them. A class that knows a wrong answer is welcome is a class willing to attempt one, which is exactly the thinking you're trying to draw out.

The strongest questioning is warm and demanding at the same time: every pupil is expected to think and answer, and every attempt is treated with respect. High expectation and high safety aren't opposites — the safety is what makes the expectation bearable.
Where it tends to go wrong

Three habits to drop.

Hands up.

It hands the lesson to the willing few and lets everyone else hide. Keep hands for pupils wanting to ask you something; for checking thinking, you choose who answers.

Naming first.

The moment a name comes before the question, the rest of the room relaxes. Question first, name last.

Taking the first right answer and moving on.

That tells you one pupil knew it. Probe — "how do you know?" — to see the reasoning and keep the room thinking.

The checklist

What a colleague would look for.

This is the self-audit we use for questioning, set out one-to-one with our own checklist and generalised. It runs in the three families this session covers. Rate each red, amber, or green.

Cold calling

CC1 · Stands in a strong position in the room.

You hold a settled, visible position as you question.

CC3 · Asks a strong, open question of the whole class, without naming anyone first.

The question has a precise answer in mind and is put to everyone.

CC4 · Builds in thinking time, judged to the question.

A real pause, gauged to what's being asked, before any answer.

CC5 · Scans the room, checking pupils are focusing on the task.

You read the room while pupils think.

CC6 · Selects a pupil to respond, by name.

Only after the question and the pause do you choose who answers.

CC7 · Other pupils are attentive to the answer.

The rest of the class listens rather than switching off.

CC8 · Acknowledges the answer, then affirms or probes further.

You respond to what's given and push for more where it helps.

CC9 · Asks for more detail or the reasoning behind the answer.

"Which method?", "how did you get there?" — reasoning made visible.

CC11 · Identifies and addresses misconceptions as they arise.

Wrong turns are spotted and put right in the moment.

CC13 · Adapts the teaching based on the responses.

What you hear changes what you do next.

Probing

PQ1 · Asks an open question inviting pupils to think deeply about the key idea.

The question is about thinking hard, not recalling a fact.

PQ2 · Selects a pupil after appropriate think time, through cold calling.

Think time first, then a named pupil.

PQ3 · Affirms the contribution; highlights the key idea or rephrases for clarity.

You acknowledge the answer and sharpen it where needed.

PQ4 · Asks a targeted follow-up to extend thinking.

Clarify, justify, use evidence, consider an alternative — the next push.

PQ7 · Invites other pupils to build on, challenge, or respond to the answer.

The probe opens out to the class rather than staying with one pupil.

Think, pair, share

TPS1 · Asks a strong, open question of the whole class, without naming anyone first.

A clear question with a precise outcome, put to everyone.

TPS2 · Indicates think time with a clear timeframe, and waits.

Pupils think on their own first, with a stated amount of time.

TPS3 · Asks pupils to discuss in pairs, with a clear timescale and outcome.

Paired talk is set up with a time limit and a reminder of the goal.

TPS4 · Circulates to listen as pairs talk — noting errors and keeping them on task.

You move and listen, gathering what pairs are saying.

TPS6 · Cold-calls one pupil to share a clear summary of their thinking.

After the talk, a named pupil gives a considered answer.

Coaching task

Re-run a question sequence.

15 minutes · in pairs

No hands, more thinking.

  1. Each take a topic and write three questions you'd normally ask the class.
  2. Rework each one as: ask the room, pause, then name a pupil — and have one follow-up ready to probe.
  3. Decide where a "talk to your partner first" would lift the quality of the answers.
  4. Run one sequence with your partner; they check you asked first and probed at least once.
