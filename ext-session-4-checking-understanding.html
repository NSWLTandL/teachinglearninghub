Finding out whether it actually landed.

The most common mistake in teaching is moving on too soon. The explanation felt clear, the room was quiet, a couple of confident pupils nodded — so the lesson presses on, and it is only at the next test that it turns out half the class never understood. Checking for understanding is the habit of finding that out while you can still do something about it, rather than weeks later. This session is the method we use, with the parts that name our school taken out.

The thing to aim for is a response from every pupil, not just the keen ones. A question answered by one volunteer tells you that one pupil understood. To teach the class actually in front of you, you need to see the whole room's thinking at once, quickly enough to act on it inside the lesson. The moves below are the practical ways to do that, and a simple cycle for the part of the lesson where most checking gets lost.

The moves

Five ways to see what they understand.

Get an answer from everyone at once.

Mini-whiteboards, hand signals, or a quick "everyone show me" turns a check you can't see into one you can. In a few seconds you see every pupil's answer, not just the volunteer's. It is the fastest way to read a whole room, and it is a leveller — every pupil has to commit to an answer rather than hide behind the confident few.

What to do: At one key moment in your next lesson, have every pupil show an answer at the same time. Read the whole room before you react to any one answer.

Move around the room on purpose.

During independent work, move on a route you've planned, so no pupil gets missed — a circuit, not a wander. You're reading who has it, who is quietly stuck, and who is finished and needs more. Working from among the desks shows you every page; staying at the front shows you almost nothing.

What to do: Plan your route before the lesson, and start moving within thirty seconds of setting the class off.

Mark it there and then.

As you move around, mark in the moment — underline the error, name the fix, and have the pupil correct it before you move on. Feedback given where the pupil is sitting, while the work is fresh, is worth far more than the same comment written in a book that night — and it means you aren't carrying thirty books home.

What to do: On your circuit, give one pupil a ten-second fix and watch them act on it before you step away.

Check before you set them off.

Before you release the class to a task, make sure they actually know what to do — a quick question to one or two, or "show me you're ready." Thirty seconds here saves ten minutes of pupils doing the wrong thing with great confidence.

What to do: Before independent work, ask one pupil to say the task back. Fix any gap before anyone starts.

Stop and re-teach when it's a common problem.

If your checks show the same mistake cropping up around the room, stop and re-teach it to everyone. Fixing it pupil by pupil is slow and lets it spread; a minute of whole-class re-teaching sorts it once. Checking is only worth doing if you act on what it tells you.

What to do: Set yourself a rule — if you see the same error three times, stop the class and re-model it.
A rhythm for independent work

Set, settle, sweep, stop.

A useful rhythm for any stretch of independent work, and the part of the lesson where checking most often gets lost. Set the task with a clear, checked instruction. Settle the class — hold your position, scan, see who has actually started. Sweep the room on your planned route, marking as you go. Stop the whole class to re-teach if a common error shows up. It gives the messy middle of a lesson a shape, instead of "set them off and hope."

Most checking opportunities go missing in exactly this stretch — after you've explained and before you review — because nothing gives it a structure. The four words are just a way of remembering to keep looking.
Where it tends to go wrong

Three things that feel like checking but aren't.

"Everyone got that?"

Only the confident answer, and always with "yes." It tells you nothing. Ask a specific question of someone who didn't volunteer, or get an answer from all of them.

Taking quiet as understanding.

A silent room might be working or might be lost. Silence isn't evidence either way. Make the thinking visible before you trust it.

Checking, then carrying on regardless.

Spotting that pupils are stuck and pressing on anyway because the plan said so. The check is wasted unless it changes what you do next.

What a colleague would look for.

Coaching task

Build one check in.

15 minutes · in pairs

Where will you look?

  1. Each take a lesson you'll teach soon. Find the one moment where understanding most needs checking.
  2. Choose how you'll check it: an answer from everyone, a checked instruction, or a planned point on your circuit.
  3. Decide in advance what you'll do if the check shows a common error — your re-teach plan.
  4. Share it with your partner; they test whether it really sees every pupil, not just some.
