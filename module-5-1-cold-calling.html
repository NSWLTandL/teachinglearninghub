Orientation

Five routines, one purpose.

Pillar 5 covers three questioning routines used at Nishkam School: cold-calling, probing questions, and think-pair-share. These combine with choral response (taught in Pillar 3) and mini-whiteboards (Pillar 4) to form the Choral → MWB → Pair → Cold-call → Probe sequence used to build, check, and develop pupil thinking. 01 · Why this matters

Cold-calling is an expectation, not a trap.

Cold-calling is the technique of asking a question to the whole class, holding thinking time, then naming an individual to answer — without hands up. Used consistently, it raises the proportion of pupils thinking at any one moment from a small group to the whole class.

Two pieces of evidence shape the technique. Mary Budd Rowe's research on wait time shows that most classroom questions are answered in under one second, but holding three seconds for a recall question and ten or more for a higher-order question produces measurably better, more thoughtful responses. Doug Lemov's work shows that hands-up routines limit participation to a confident minority, and that "the magic lies in the pause": held thinking time creates anticipation and signals that thinking is expected of everyone.

Cold-calling should not feel like a trap. It should feel like an expectation. In a high-functioning classroom, all students expect to be called on because thinking is expected of everyone.Doug Lemov · Teach Like a Champion

The cold-call sequence below is delivered in the same order every time. The order is what makes it work: question first, pause second, name third. Reversed, the technique is hands-up by another route.

1
Step 1 · Set the room

Strong position. Diligence triggered.

CC1

The teacher takes the strong position — front-centre, body still — and triggers diligence using the Module 1.3 routine. Pens down, equipment down, eyes on the teacher. The whole class is attentive before the first question is asked.

Cold-calling delivered into a half-attentive class produces half-attentive answers. The two seconds spent on the diligence trigger here are recovered five times over in the quality of thinking the pupils then bring to the question.

Scripted language"Peacefulness, diligence — pens down, eyes on me, in three, two, one." PAUSE · SCAN
Wait for the room. The cold-call sequence runs from a settled room or it does not run. Do not begin the question until every pupil is in the diligent state.
2
Step 2 · Ask the whole class

Question first. No name.

CC3CC4

The question is posed to the whole class with no name attached. The phrasing is brief and unambiguous. The teacher does not say "I'm going to ask someone in a moment" or "anyone?" — these phrasings convert the cold-call back into a hands-up question by signalling that an individual is being identified up front.

Clarity and economy of language are the standards. A long, qualified question loses pupils before they can begin thinking; a short, direct question gives every pupil access to the same thinking task.

Scripted language"What is the first phase of mitosis?"
No name yet. CC4 is explicit: no name attached to the initial question. The pupil is selected only after the pause.
3
Step 3 · Hold the pause · the technique

Hold the pause. Scan the room.

CC5

The pause is the technique. Three seconds for a recall question; ten seconds or more for a higher-order question. The teacher holds the pause without filling it — no rephrasing, no clarification, no "anyone?". The room is scanned during the pause to check that pupils are thinking, not just looking.

CC5 specifies that the duration of the pause is gauged for the purpose of the question. Recall questions take three seconds because the answer is being retrieved from memory; higher-order questions take longer because the answer is being constructed. A teacher whose pauses are uniformly short is asking only recall questions, regardless of how the questions are worded.

Scripted language( silence — 3 seconds for recall · 10 seconds for higher-order ) SCAN · WAIT
The pause is held without filling it. The room is scanned. Pupils who are not thinking are noticed silently for later attention; the silence is not broken to address them.
Coaching moment3 minutes · in pairs
  1. The partner generates one recall question and one higher-order question on a topic the teacher knows.
  2. The teacher delivers each in full, holding three seconds for the first and ten for the second. The partner times silently with a watch.
  3. The partner reports whether the pauses were held to length and whether the teacher's body language remained settled across the pause.
Coach prompt: Did the teacher fill the silence with rephrasing or filler words? Did the body language read as calm thinking time, or as awkward waiting?
4
Step 4 · Name the pupil

One pupil. Briefly named.

CC6

After the pause, the teacher names a single pupil and directs the question to them. The naming is brief and unambiguous: "[Pupil]." The question is not re-asked unless the pupil indicates they did not hear it.

The choice of pupil is not random. Across a half-term, the teacher tracks who has been called on and aims for an even distribution. A pupil who has not been called on in three lessons is more likely to be called on next; a pupil who has answered correctly in the previous question is unlikely to be called on for the immediate next one.

Scripted language( name only ) "[Pupil]."
5
Step 5 · Acknowledge & extend

Acknowledge the answer. Build on it.

CC8CC9

The pupil's answer is acknowledged briefly — affirmed where correct, rephrased where partially correct, probed where requiring more detail. The teacher does not move immediately to the next question; the answer is engaged with before the lesson advances.

Two extensions are routine. CC9: ask the same pupil for more detail or for the reasoning behind the answer ("How did you reach that?"). CC10: invite a second pupil to extend, build on, or challenge the first pupil's answer ("[Second pupil], can you add to that?"). Module 5.2 covers the probing follow-up sequence in full.

Scripted language"Good — that's prophase. PAUSE How did you remember that one? PAUSE [Second pupil], can you add the next phase?"
6
Step 6 · Adapt

Adapt teaching based on the response.

CC11CC13CC14

The cold-call sequence ends in a teaching decision: move on, reteach, or run a probing follow-up. Misconceptions revealed by the pupil's answer are addressed at the point of the misconception rather than later. Where the pupil's answer reveals that the misconception is shared by the class, a brief reteach precedes the next question.

CC14 is the bridge to Module 5.2. Where the pupil has given a correct answer at surface level, the teacher continues with probing questions to extend the response and to surface the underlying reasoning. Cold-calling delivers the answer; probing develops the thinking.

Read the room before the next question. If three pupils in a row have given partial or incorrect answers to similar questions, the issue is the teaching, not the pupils. Reteach before the next question.
Coaching moment · full sequence5 minutes · in trios
  1. The trio agrees a topic and two questions: one recall, one higher-order.
  2. The teacher runs the full six-step sequence end-to-end on each question.
  3. The observer holds the CC checklist open and ticks each item as it lands. The "pupil" answers with one slight error to give the teacher something to acknowledge and probe.
  4. Roles rotate after each full sequence.
Coach prompt: Were Steps 3 and 5 — the held pause and the acknowledgement — given the time they need, or were they rushed past?
03 · Pitfalls

Where the cold-call breaks.

Each pitfall is tagged with the checklist item it puts at risk.

Naming the pupil before the pause.Risks CC4, CC5

"[Pupil], what is…" — the rest of the class is now off the hook because the question is not for them. The technique is question first, pause, name third. The order is non-negotiable.

Filling the pause.Risks CC5

The teacher rephrases the question or adds "anyone?" during the pause. The thinking time is destroyed. Three seconds in silence produce more thinking than thirty seconds of teacher talk.

Same pause length for every question.Risks CC5

Three seconds for both recall and higher-order. The higher-order questions are answered as if they were recall, and the deeper thinking the question was designed to elicit does not occur.

Calling on the same pupils.Risks CC-O1

The teacher returns to a small group of pupils whose hands would otherwise have been up. The remaining 80 per cent of the class registers that cold-calling is selective and stops thinking. The technique requires a tracked spread across the class.

Dropping the answer.Risks CC8, CC9

The pupil answers; the teacher says "good" and moves to the next question. The answer has been received but not engaged with, and the pupil registers that the contribution was not valued. Acknowledge briefly, then either probe or move to a second pupil.

Cold-calling into chaos.Risks CC1

The teacher launches a cold-call sequence while the room is still settling. The first pupil is named while half the class is not yet listening. The diligence trigger is run first.

05 · AI scenario tester

Worked example.

The full live tool will accept any scenario the teacher describes. The example below shows the shape of a typical response.

Worked example

Scenario tester

Teacher submits
"My [Year 10] class — the same five pupils answer everything. I've started cold-calling but I'm reaching for the same names. When I do call on a quiet pupil they say 'I don't know'. I move on to one of the usual five and the lesson runs but it feels like the cold-call is a formality."
Tool responds

Items at risk: CC4 (likely no genuine pause; pupil identified mentally before question is asked), CC5 (pause may be uniformly short — three seconds for everything), CC-O1 (selection is predictable, so pupils not on the teacher's list disengage).

What is happening: the cold-call is being run as hands-up with the names selected silently rather than by the pupils. The "I don't know" response from quiet pupils is the pattern of pupils who have not been thinking because they have learned that selection is not random.

Across the next two weeks:

  1. Track who has been called on. Print a class list; tick once per question. Aim for every pupil ticked at least twice across each lesson.
  2. Lengthen Step 3. Time the pause on a watch — three seconds for recall, ten for higher-order. The pupils who currently say "I don't know" need the longer pause to retrieve.
  3. For the "I don't know" response, do not move on. Use the no-opt-out sequence: ask a second pupil for the answer, then return to the original pupil and ask them to repeat it. The original pupil now has the answer and has been heard giving it. The norm shifts within a fortnight.
  4. Plan the questions in advance — set pieces, not improvised. Where each cold-call has a planned target pupil and a planned probing follow-up, the technique runs more reliably.

To avoid: giving up on cold-calling because the early attempts feel uncomfortable. The "I don't know" response is part of the embedding period, not a failure of the technique.
