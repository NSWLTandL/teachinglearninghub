The Inclusive Classroom.

Inclusion begins with the room and the routines that run it. A calm, predictable environment, and additional adults deployed to build independence, remove barriers before a lesson even starts.

Orientation

Three modules, one pillar.

Pillar 2 has three modules: the inclusive classroom, adaptive teaching in the lesson, and the SEND intervention. This first module is about the conditions every other adaptation depends on, the calm and predictable room.

01 · Why this matters

The room is the first adaptation.

Pupils who find school hardest are the ones a cluttered, unpredictable, over-stimulating room fails first. Getting the environment and routines right removes barriers before any lesson content is taught.

This is also where additional adults do their most important work. The aim is always to build a pupil's independence, never to seat an adult beside them doing the work for them.

The EEF is clear that the everyday classroom environment and consistent routines are the foundation on which all other support for pupils with additional needs rests.after the EEF · Special Educational Needs in Mainstream Schools
02 · The sequence

From the room to the adult.

03 · In practice

Holding the conditions.

Three habits keep the inclusive classroom in place day to day.

  1. Keep the room calm and uncluttered, with displays that support rather than distract, and a layout arranged for the pupils in front of you.
  2. Make routines, visual prompts and timetables genuinely consistent, and where something must change, prepare the pupils who find change hardest.
  3. Brief any additional adult on the lesson in advance, and agree how they will build independence rather than do the work for a pupil.
04 · Pitfalls

What goes wrong, and the fix.

SP2Routines that change without warning. The pupils who most need predictability are the ones a last-minute change unsettles most.
SP8The additional adult who becomes a permanent prop. Support that never fades quietly teaches a pupil they cannot manage alone.
