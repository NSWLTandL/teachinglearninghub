Leading when it is hard

The real test of leadership is the difficult conversation. This area is about the shift to a leader’s lens, and the craft of feedback and accountability that build trust rather than erode it.

“Avoiding difficult conversations erodes trust; having them with clarity builds it.”

The shift

The leadership lens

Middle leadership is not just a bigger teaching role; it is a shift in how you see your work — a wider lens on people, systems, learning, and culture. Even tough moments are an opportunity to lead.

Teacher lensLeader lens
“How can I make this lesson work?”“How can I help all lessons in my team work better?”
“How am I supporting my pupils?”“How is my team supporting their pupils?”
“How can I manage my workload?”“How can I support the team to manage theirs?”
“Why are they not doing what’s expected?”“Have I been clear, consistent, and followed up fairly?”
“What CPD do I need?”“What CPD will move the team forward?”

Notice which lens you default to under pressure — and how you might shift it.

Courage

Difficult conversations that build trust

Difficult conversations are not optional. Avoiding them erodes trust; having them with clarity builds credibility.

Doug Lemov — ‘precise praise’ and a ‘warm-strict’ approach — high expectations held alongside strong relationships — let you give honest feedback that motivates rather than alienates.
Patterson et al. (Crucial Conversations) — the most effective leaders stay calm under pressure, focus on facts rather than personal judgements, and seek a shared purpose.
Jim Knight — accountability works when paired with empathy and a genuine belief in the other person’s potential.
Stone & Heen (Thanks for the Feedback) — people act on feedback when they trust the motive, it is specific not general, it targets behaviour not personality, and it offers a clear path forward.

The hardest part is the first step. A few well-chosen sentence starters help you strike a tone that is professional, respectful, and direct.

I’ve noticed… and I wanted to talk about it.
Can we talk about something that might be a bit tricky?
I wanted to check in about… because it links to our shared expectations.
Help me understand how you’re feeling about…
Prepare

The conversation planner

Before a high-stakes conversation, think it through. Five questions to plan from.

What is the core outcome you need from this conversation?

Be clear on the purpose: raise a concern, clarify expectations, agree a plan, or address a pattern.

How do you want the other person to leave feeling?

Aim for respectful, supported, and clear — not defensive or demoralised.

What evidence will you bring?

Use specific, factual examples. Avoid generalisations and assumptions.

What objections might come up, and how will you respond?

Plan calm, constructive replies that keep the conversation on track.

What tone and body language will keep it calm?

Open posture, measured pace, neutral but firm language. Model the professionalism you expect.

Feedback that lands is direct, constructive, and kind. A simple structure: what was seen (objective and specific), why it matters (link to learning and expectations), and what could change (clear and actionable).

Reflect

Lead the moment

  1. Where do you already think like a leader? Where do you still default to the classroom-teacher lens, and why?
  2. What stops people from having difficult conversations — and what are the risks of not addressing things?
  3. Think of one conversation you have been putting off. What is the first sentence you will use?
