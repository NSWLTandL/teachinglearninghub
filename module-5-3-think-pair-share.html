Orientation

Five routines, one purpose.

This module covers pair-share — the brief paired discussion that gives pupils thinking time and an opportunity to rehearse an answer before saying it to the class. Pair-share is the transition routine in the Choral → MWB → Pair → Cold-call → Probe sequence.

01 · Why this matters

Rehearsal before performance.

Pair-share solves a specific problem: some pupils need to talk through an answer before they can say it to the class. Cold-calling alone produces freezes from those pupils; pair-share preceding cold-calling produces fluent answers from the same pupils.

The technique is brief — typically thirty to ninety seconds of paired talk — and is used selectively. Pair-share is not the default questioning routine; it is the routine the teacher reaches for when the question needs rehearsal time, when confidence in the room is low, or when the next move is a cold-call into deeper material.

The discriminating standard is that pair-share is bounded by routine — diligence triggered before, time-limited during, diligence triggered again after. An open-ended pair-share with no terminating signal is a chat with no productive end, and the class learns to treat the routine as off-task time rather than as rehearsal time.

Don't rush. Let students retrieve the answer individually first — this strengthens memory. Then they can discuss with a partner. I usually suggest a full minute for thinking before sharing. That helps them form their ideas and find the words to express them.Dylan Wiliam · Effective Questioning in the Classroom

Step 1 · Pose the question

Whole class. Open phrasing. No name.

CC3CC4

The question is asked of the whole class — open phrasing, no name attached. The pupil knows that after the pair-share they may be called on, and the partner knows the same; both are accountable for arriving at an answer they could share.

Pair-share is most productive when the question requires rehearsal — a "what would happen if" or "compare X and Y" question rather than a recall question. Recall belongs to choral response (Module 3.2) or mini-whiteboards (Module 4.1); pair-share is for questions where pupils benefit from talking the answer out before producing it.

Scripted language"Compare the responsibility of the two characters for what happens at the end of the chapter." PAUSE · 3 SEC
2
Step 2 · Set the time and pairs

Bounded talk. Timed.

The duration is named in advance. Thirty seconds for a brief rehearsal; sixty seconds for a comparison or evaluation; ninety seconds where the question requires more development. The teacher does not extend the time once the discussion has begun — the time is a contract, not a suggestion.

Pairs are pre-assigned by seating. The teacher does not negotiate "who's your partner" at the start of each pair-share; the seating plan determines the pairs and the routine begins immediately. Where a pupil is absent, the adjacent triple operates for the duration.

Scripted language"With your partner — sixty seconds. PAUSE Go."
3
Step 3 · Talk time · the technique

Teacher circulates. Listens. Does not interject.

During the pair-talk, the teacher circulates and listens silently. The information collected is the same as during MWB silent thinking: which pairs are productive, which are off-task, which are converging on a wrong answer the teacher will want to address, and which pupils are producing a strong answer the teacher will want to call on first.

The teacher does not interject. Pair-share is the rehearsal — the pupils need the time to work the answer out together. A teacher who joins each pair turns the rehearsal into instruction, and the answers the pupils produce in Step 4 are not their own.

The teacher is silent during pair-share. Listen. Note who to call on first. The answers that come back in Step 4 are then real answers the teacher can probe, not echoes of the teacher's hint.
Coaching moment5 minutes · in trios
  1. One colleague plays the teacher; two play paired pupils. The teacher sets a sixty-second pair-share on a topic the "pupils" know.
  2. The pupils talk audibly, with one going slightly off-topic at the thirty-second mark.
  3. The teacher circulates silently for the full sixty seconds without interjecting.
  4. At sixty seconds, the teacher triggers diligence ("3-2-1, eyes on me") and names which pupil they would call on first and why.
Coach prompt: Was the teacher silent throughout? Did the choice of first-called pupil match what the listening had revealed?
4
Step 4 · Diligence and select

Reset the room. Cold-call.

CC6

At the named time, the teacher triggers diligence — the Module 1.3 routine. Pens down, eyes on me, in three, two, one. The pair-share ends cleanly; the room transitions back to whole-class attention before the next move.

The teacher then cold-calls — Module 5.1 from Step 4 onwards. The pupil selected is named; the answer is acknowledged; the probe (Module 5.2) follows where the answer warrants depth. The pair-share has done its work: the named pupil is now answering with a rehearsed response rather than a frozen one.

Scripted language"Three, two, one — eyes on me. PAUSE · SCAN [Pupil] — what did you and your partner come up with?"
03 · The integrating cycle

Choral → MWB → Pair → Cold-call → Probe.

Five routines drawn from across the pillars — choral response and mini-whiteboards from checking, and the three questioning routines — combine into a single sequence the teacher uses to build understanding from listening to deeper thinking. Not every lesson runs the full cycle. The teacher chooses the entry and exit points by purpose. A retrieval check at the start of the lesson is Choral → MWB and stops there. A new-concept introduction is MWB (hinge) → reteach. A discussion-based question is Pair → Cold-call → Probe. The full cycle is reserved for the central concept of the lesson, where the teacher wants to move from "do they know this" to "can they reason about this".

The transition between stages is the teaching skill. Diligence is triggered between every stage so the room resets before the next routine begins; the questioning is not a continuous flow but a series of bounded routines connected by Module 1.3.

04 · Worked sequence

One concept, full cycle.

The example below shows a Year 9 English teacher using the full Choral → MWB → Pair → Cold-call → Probe sequence on a single concept — the use of caesura in the poem 'Kamikaze'.

StageWhat the teacher doesWhat pupils do
a · Choral"Together — what is a caesura?" Pupils respond in unison: "A pause in the middle of a line."Choral response. Cognitive load minimal — retrieving a known definition.
b · MWBProjects a line from the poem. "On your boards — circle the caesura. 3-2-1 Show me." Scans every board.Each pupil applies the definition to a specific text. The teacher diagnoses who can and cannot.
c · Pair-share"With your partner — sixty seconds — what is the effect of the caesura on the meaning of this line?"Pupils talk through the analytical step. Rehearse the language they will use.
d · Cold-call"3-2-1, eyes on me. [Pupil] — what did you come up with?"Named pupil answers a rehearsed response, not a frozen one.
e · Probe"Why does the caesura matter at this point in the poem rather than at the start? Pause. [Second pupil] — would you agree?"The class moves from definition to analysis to evaluation. New learning surfaces.
05 · Pitfalls

Where pair-share breaks.

Each pitfall is tagged with the item or routine it puts at risk.

Open-ended talk time.Risks the routine itself

"Talk to your partner about this for a minute or two." Pupils calibrate to the upper end and chatter beyond. The duration is named precisely and held.

No diligence trigger after.Risks the routine

The teacher tries to cold-call into the residue of paired talk. Half the room is still finishing their sentences. Diligence is triggered before the cold-call — the routine resets the room.

Pair-sharing recall questions.Misuse of routine

"Talk to your partner — what year did the war start?" The answer does not need rehearsal. Use choral or MWB instead; pair-share for questions that require thinking time.

Joining each pair.Risks the technique

The teacher visits each pair and steers the answer. The cold-call afterwards is the teacher's answer in the pupil's mouth. Listen silently; let the rehearsal be the pupils'.

Skipping the cold-call.Risks accountability

"Did everyone get something? Right, moving on." No pupil has been heard. The pair-share was off-task time, not rehearsal time. Always cold-call after.

No partner assigned.Risks the routine

Pupils negotiate partners at the start of each pair-share. Thirty seconds lost; some pupils excluded. Partners are pre-assigned by seating; the routine begins immediately.
