Session 1 · Self-study

What is a middle leader?

Middle leaders are the bridge between strategy and classroom practice. This area sets out what the role really asks of you, and the habits that make it work — to read on your own, check yourself against, and put into practice this week.

“Middle leaders don’t just deliver outcomes — they shape the conditions in which outcomes become possible.”

The role

The bridge between strategy and practice

A middle leader sits between the school’s direction and the day-to-day work of a team. Success rests on four things you do at once.

  • Lead teaching and learning in your phase or subject.
  • Influence the culture of your team, for the better.
  • Hold others to account with clarity and care.
  • Balance priorities while managing both people and systems.

“Leadership is not about being in charge. It is about taking care of those in your charge.”

What makes it work

The six C’s of a middle leader

Six qualities sit underneath effective middle leadership. Each is a habit you can practise, not a trait you either have or don’t. Under each is the move that turns it into action.

Credibility

Rooted in expert teaching, modelling, and knowing your phase or subject.

How to show it

  • Keep teaching well, visibly.
  • Model the standard you ask for.

Clarity

About priorities, standards, and the ‘why’ behind decisions.

How to show it

  • Name what good looks like.
  • Give the reason, not just the rule.

Consistency

In following up, applying policies, and treating people fairly.

How to show it

  • Do what you said you would.
  • Apply the standard to everyone.

Communication

Upward, downward, and sideways — building trust at all levels.

How to show it

  • Tell people early, and plainly.
  • Listen before you decide.

Courage

To challenge underperformance, and to say what needs saying.

How to show it

  • Name the issue, kindly and directly.
  • Don’t walk past the small slips.

Coaching mindset

Developing others rather than doing it all yourself.

How to show it

  • Ask ‘what would you try?’
  • Delegate something real.
The Nishkam lens

Leading the Nishkam way

Our leadership is rooted in service, not status. Four ideas shape how we lead.

We work for others. Leadership is service, not position.
We consider others in everything. Every decision weighs its effect on pupils, staff, and families.
We approach life with optimism. Positivity builds momentum.
We look for joy. Joy is contagious, and it builds resilient teams.
What the evidence says

Where leadership makes the difference

Viviane Robinson — the strongest impact on pupil outcomes comes from leaders who focus on improving teaching and learning.
Tom Sherrington — effective middle leaders are ‘expert teachers leading expert teachers’; curriculum, pedagogy and subject knowledge are the heartbeat.
Doug Lemov — leadership needs systems that reduce ambiguity: ‘what we permit, we promote.’
Steve Radcliffe — leadership is Future, Engage, Deliver: set a vision, build buy-in, then deliver consistently.

When things go wrong — the Nishkam response: step back (detachment); nothing is as bad as it first appears (perspective); identify what actually happened (reflection); seek advice early (support); be visible, calm and consistent (presence); and remember that how you lead shapes how others behave (role model).
