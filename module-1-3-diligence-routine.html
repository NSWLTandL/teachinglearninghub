01 · Why this matters

The Diligence Routine in context.

The Diligence Routine is the in-lesson sequence the teacher uses to move a class from independent work back to attention. It is run many times in every lesson — before a new instruction, before a worked example, before a transition to new content, before a check for understanding.

The routine builds on the language of virtues established in Module 1.1 and the lesson-start sequence in Module 1.2. It is built around a single trigger word, "diligence", and a short calibrated sequence of five beats that follow it. Pupils are taught the meaning of the word in their first weeks at Nishkam — pens down, arms folded, eyes on the teacher — so that by the time the trigger word is used in a lesson, the response it cues is already established. The teacher does not need to explain the meaning each time; saying the word and adding the definition tends to weaken the routine, because it implies that the word alone is insufficient.

Consistency is the central principle. Every teacher in the school runs the same five beats in the same order, with the same calibrated pauses between them. By Year 8, pupils have rehearsed this rhythm thousands of times and respond to it automatically. The routine works because pupils recognise the sequence, not because they are afraid of the consequences of ignoring it; this depends on every teacher delivering the routine in the same form, every time it is used.

Module 1.3 sits alongside Modules 1.1 and 1.2 in the daily operation of a Nishkam classroom. Module 1.1 supplies the vocabulary; Module 1.2 establishes the daily ritual; Module 1.3 covers the in-lesson routine that runs throughout. When the Diligence Routine is securely embedded, the warning ladder in Module 1.8 becomes a rarely-used last resort rather than a regularly-deployed tool.

02 · The five-beat rhythm

The full routine.

The routine consists of five beats run in sequence: trigger, pause, scan, instruct, release. End to end it takes approximately fifteen seconds, the majority of which is silence rather than speech.

The dashes between beats represent silence — the time the teacher leaves for pupils to respond to what has just been done. Pause durations are not optional. They are calibrated to the working speed of an attentive secondary class, and shortening them tends to break the routine. The most common cause of routine failure is the teacher filling the pause with speech or gesture before the pupils have finished responding to the previous beat.

1
Trigger

The single word, spoken from a strong position.

0:00
ChecklistBFL3

The routine begins with one word, "diligence", spoken at normal volume from a strong position in the room. Pupils have been taught the meaning of the word since Year 7: pens down, arms folded, eyes on the teacher. The teacher does not restate the meaning, because saying the word and the definition together teaches the class that the word alone is insufficient — which is the lesson the routine is designed not to teach.

The technique
The teacher stands in a strong position, delivers the word at normal volume, and then stops speaking. The word is not repeated, not qualified, and not followed up. Once the word has been delivered, the routine has begun and the next beat is silence.
Scripted language"Diligence."
Deliberate practice60 seconds · in pairs
  1. The teacher stands in a strong position and waits two seconds before speaking. The silence before the word is part of the trigger.
  2. The teacher delivers the word at normal volume and then stops speaking.
  3. Run five repetitions. After each, the partner reports whether the word was weighted or rushed, and whether anything followed it.
Coach prompt: Was the word delivered at normal volume, or did it rise to fill the room? Did the teacher stop after the word, or did they qualify it ("diligence — pens down, please…")?
If you have failed BFL3

The fix is almost always vocal. Record the trigger word delivered once, then five times in succession. The recording reveals whether delivery is consistent in volume and weight across repetitions.

2
Pause

Three seconds of silence while the room responds.

+0:01 → +0:04
ChecklistBFL4

After the trigger word, the teacher says nothing for three seconds. This is the active part of the routine: pupils close pens, fold arms, and turn their eyes to the front. The pause is the time given for them to do so. If the teacher fills the silence, the routine collapses — pupils receive a second instruction before they have completed the first, and the trigger word loses its weight in subsequent uses.

Three seconds is longer than it feels in the moment. It is calibrated to the working speed of an attentive class and is not negotiable.

The technique
The teacher's mouth is closed, hands are still, and body is still. The teacher does not nod, gesture, or reissue the word. An internal three-count is acceptable while the routine is being established. The pause is the instruction at this stage of the sequence.
Deliberate practice90 seconds · in pairs
  1. One colleague delivers the trigger word; the other times the silence on a watch.
  2. The teacher holds three full seconds without speaking, gesturing, or moving.
  3. Run five repetitions. The timer reports whether the pause shrank as the routine repeated, or held its full duration each time.
Coach prompt: Did the teacher hold the full three seconds? When the pause was shortened, what filled it — a word, a gesture, a tightening of the body?
If you have failed BFL4 (pause half)

This is the foundation skill of the routine. Arrange for a coaching partner to time the pause in real lessons across one week. The data alone resolves most cases.

3
Scan

A visible scan of the room, with three or four explicit eye-meets.

+0:04 → +0:07
ChecklistBFL4

After the silent pause, the teacher visibly scans the room — corner to corner, with three or four explicit eye-meets including any pupils who are not yet compliant. The scan tells the class that compliance is being measured, and the eye-meet is the correction in cases where compliance has not yet occurred. A second instruction is not required at this point. Without the scan, the routine reduces to a request, and pupils learn over time that intermittent compliance is acceptable.

The technique
The scan uses visible head movement, not eye flicks alone. Three or four explicit fixations are made across the room, including the back row and the corners. Where a pupil is not yet compliant, the teacher holds gaze for half a second; in most cases this resolves the issue without a second word.
Deliberate practice60 seconds · in trios
  1. Two colleagues stand at opposite corners of the room as pupils.
  2. The teacher delivers the trigger, holds the pause, then scans — visibly meeting the eyes of each pupil in turn.
  3. The pupils report whether they felt seen, and whether the scan covered the whole room or only the part nearest the teacher.
Coach prompt: Could the pupils tell when they were being looked at? Did the teacher scan the whole room, or only the part nearest to them?
If you have failed BFL4 (scan half)

Rehearse the scan in front of a mirror. The head movement should be deliberate enough to be visible to an observer at the back of the room.

4
Instruct

The instruction, delivered in performance order, in three steps or fewer.

+0:07 → +0:12
ChecklistBFL5-ABFL5-B

At this point in the sequence — and not before — the teacher gives the instruction. It is delivered in performance order: pupils hear it in the sequence in which they will perform it. It contains no more than three steps, which corresponds to the working memory limit of an attentive secondary class. Four steps reaches the threshold of forgetting and produces partial compliance.

The technique
Steps are sequenced in the order pupils will perform them. The same connective vocabulary is used in every lesson — "first... then... finally." Where a class finds the sequence hard, the teacher anchors the steps tactilely by counting them off on the fingers as they are spoken.
Scripted language — example"First, turn to page 47. Then, read the first paragraph silently. Finally, write the date and title in your book."
Deliberate practice2 minutes · in pairs
  1. One colleague is the teacher; the other holds a stopwatch and a pen.
  2. The teacher delivers a three-step instruction in performance order.
  3. The pupil writes down what they heard. The pair compare the written version against the original — were the three steps preserved, and were they in performance order?
  4. Repeat with three different scenarios: starter task, transition between activities, end-of-lesson pack-up.
Coach prompt: Did the instruction contain more than three steps? Were they in performance order, or in the order they occurred to the teacher?
If you have failed BFL5-A or BFL5-B

The teacher plans their three highest-frequency instructions in writing before the lesson. The instructions are written out word for word, in performance order, on a sticky note on the lectern. For the first week, the teacher reads from the note as the instruction is delivered.

5
Release

The release word that begins pupil action.

+0:12 → +0:14
ChecklistBFL6

The instruction ends with a single trigger word that releases the class to act: "go". Pupils begin only on the trigger. They have been taught since Year 7 that no instruction is live until the release word arrives, which protects the silence between the trigger and the release as a working part of the routine rather than a gap to be filled.

The pause between the last step of the instruction and "go" carries weight in the sequence: it tells pupils that the instruction is complete and the release is incoming. Where this pause is shortened, pupils begin acting at random points during the instruction rather than at a single moment afterwards.

The technique
The teacher holds a one-second pause after the final step, then says "go" at normal volume. A second scan follows the release word, which gives the teacher information about whether the instruction was followed first time.
Scripted language"...write the date and title in your book. — Pause. — Go."
Deliberate practice90 seconds · in pairs
  1. The teacher delivers a three-step instruction, holds the one-second pause, then says "go".
  2. The pupil reports the moment they began acting in their head — was it on "go", or earlier?
  3. Repeat. The aim is for the pupil to begin exactly on "go", neither before nor after.
Coach prompt: Was the pause before "go" actually held? Did the teacher say "go" with the same weight as "diligence", or did it slip into the end of the instruction?
If you have failed BFL6

The most common failure is omitting the release word entirely; the teacher gives the instruction and pupils have to guess when to begin. The fix is to add the trigger word back into the sequence. The release word is a routine step, not an optional one.

03 · End-to-end practice

Running the full rhythm.

The routine is short enough to be rehearsed many times in succession within a single deliberate practice session. Repetition is what produces the automaticity the routine depends on for its effect.

FEATURED PRACTICE · 5 MINUTES · IN TRIOS

Ten repetitions in five minutes.

Trios consist of one teacher and two pupils. The trio runs the full five-beat routine ten times in succession, with a different instruction each time. By the tenth repetition, the rhythm should be automatic.

  1. Set: the trio agrees ten plausible mid-lesson instructions in advance — a mixture of starter calls, transitions between activities, transitions back from talk to silence, and end-of-lesson pack-ups.
  2. Run: the teacher executes the full routine — trigger, pause, scan, instruct, release — ten times, with one of the agreed instructions each time. There is no stopping between repetitions.
  3. Score: the pupils hold a tally. For each repetition, they mark whether each of the five beats landed cleanly. The total is out of fifty marks per teacher.
  4. Rotate: roles are swapped and the activity repeats. The second teacher's score is typically higher than the first's by repetitions six to ten, which is the effect of having observed the previous round.
Coach prompt: Of the fifty possible marks, how many landed? Which beat had the lowest hit rate — pause, scan, or release? That is the beat to drill before the next lesson.
04 · Common pitfalls

Where the routine breaks.

Each pitfall below is tagged with the checklist item it puts at risk. The diagnostic value of the pitfall list lies in pairing the failure mode with the specific item that will fall to amber or red as a consequence.

Filling the pause.Risks BFL4

The teacher delivers the trigger word and immediately follows it with elaboration ("diligence — pens down, please — eyes on me"). Each addition undermines the trigger word, because pupils learn that the word alone is insufficient to begin the routine. The correct action is to deliver the word and hold the silence.

Rising volume on the trigger.Risks BFL3

The trigger word is delivered louder on each successive use. Volume escalation teaches pupils that the routine bends under pressure and that compliance is required only when the teacher is sufficiently insistent. The trigger word is delivered at the same volume every time it is used.

Skipping the scan.Risks BFL4

The teacher delivers the trigger and pause, then moves directly to the instruction without a scan. Without the scan, the routine functions as a request rather than a check, and pupils learn over time that intermittent compliance is acceptable.

Four-step instructions.Risks BFL5-B

The teacher delivers an instruction with four or more steps ("turn to page 47, read the first paragraph, write the date and title, and answer question one"). Working memory will not hold four steps reliably. The correct action is to split the instruction into two routines, or to trim it to three steps.

Wrong-order instructions.Risks BFL5-A

The teacher delivers steps in a sequence other than performance order ("answer question one once you have written the date and title and read the paragraph"). The cognitive cost of reordering steps in real time is high, and partial compliance is the typical result. Performance order is used every time.

Implied release.Risks BFL6

The teacher delivers the instruction and adopts an expectant posture without saying "go". Pupils begin acting at random points. The correct action is to add the explicit release word back into the routine; the release is a routine step rather than something pupils are expected to infer.

Using "diligence" when a different routine is needed.Risks BFL3

The trigger word is for transitions to attention. Where the room needs calm rather than focus, the appropriate word is "peacefulness" (Module 1.1). Where it needs a full stop and reset, the appropriate move is the warning ladder (Module 1.8). Diligence is not a substitute for either.

06 · AI scenario tester

Worked example of a tool response.

The full live tool will accept any scenario the teacher describes. The worked example below shows the shape of a typical response.

Worked example

Scenario tester

Teacher submits
"It's twenty minutes into a [Year 8] lesson. Pupils are working on a paired discussion. I need to bring them back to the front for the next instruction. I called 'diligence' but the volume in the room only dipped — half the class kept talking. I called it again, louder. Most stopped, but two tables didn't, so I went over to them. Now five minutes have gone and I haven't given the next instruction yet."
Tool responds

Items at risk: BFL3 (trigger word degraded by repetition and rising volume), BFL4 (pause not held; scan replaced with proximity).

What's going wrong: the second, louder "diligence" has taught the room that the first one was negotiable. The proximity walk to the chatty tables has resolved the immediate problem but reinforced the underlying one — pupils now have evidence that ignoring the trigger word brings the teacher to them, which is more attention rather than less.

What to do next time:

  1. Deliver the trigger word from a strong position, at normal volume, and then close your mouth.
  2. Hold three full seconds of silence and time it on the clock. Most of the class will respond within that window; some will not.
  3. Run a visible scan with three or four eye-meets, holding gaze on the chatty tables for half a second each. Add no words.
  4. If the chatty tables still talk after the scan, that is a Module 1.8 issue (warning ladder) rather than a Module 1.3 issue, and the appropriate move is to switch routines.
  5. If they comply by the end of the scan, deliver the instruction in three steps, hold the one-second pause, and then say "go".

What not to do: avoid delivering the trigger word a second time. Once "diligence" has been issued, the routine is running, and reissuing the trigger word communicates that the first delivery was not sufficient. The fix lives in the scan and, where necessary, in the warning ladder of Module 1.8.
