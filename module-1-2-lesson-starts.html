01 · Why this matters

The first three minutes set the rest.

A lesson that opens with calm purpose almost never goes off the rails. A lesson that opens with drift almost never recovers.

The lesson start is the most pedagogically dense part of the day. In three minutes the teacher must establish presence in the corridor, greet a class one pupil at a time, transition forty bodies from social to academic, deliver a virtue-based reset, run a thirty-second silent reflection, set a starter task, and take the register — without raising their voice once.

This is not personality. It is sequence. The teachers whose lessons begin well do not have charisma the others lack. They have a routine the others have not yet rehearsed. The nine stages below are that routine. Each is small. Each is scripted. Each is rehearsable.

Great behaviour isn't built on charisma or control — it's built on consistent habits that make attention and effort the norm. Doug Lemov, Teach Like a Champion

This module assumes you have completed Module 1.1 — The Language of Virtues. The mantra, the named virtues, and the "we" form of correction all appear in what follows. If any of those phrases feel unfamiliar, return to 1.1 before continuing.

Stage 1 of 9 · Before the bell

The corridor.

The lesson starts before any pupil has crossed your threshold. Where you are standing — and how — is the first piece of teaching of the period.

1
Corridor

Be present. Be visible. Be the calm.

−2 minutes

The teacher is in the corridor before pupils arrive. Standing — not sitting at a computer, not finishing a marking pile. Visible. Calm. The corridor is the first setting in which behaviour is shaped, and it is shaped by adults who are seen there.

At Nishkam School, pupils move through the building on the left-hand side, in single file. The teacher reinforces this without raised voice — by being there, by gesturing, by naming a pupil who is doing it well.

The technique
Stand at the threshold of your classroom. Body angled so you can see both the corridor and the room. Speak to pupils as they pass — not to control, but to greet. The presence is the message.
Scripted language "Morning, [Pupil]. Left-hand side, please. — Thank you."
Deliberate practice 60 seconds · in pairs
  1. One colleague stands at an imaginary threshold; the other walks past as a pupil.
  2. The "teacher" greets, redirects, names a virtue (one is enough).
  3. Switch.
Coach prompt: Was the body open and present, or were they leaning, looking at a phone, retreating into the room? Did the greeting acknowledge the pupil specifically?
2
Greeting

Direct them in. Indicate the task.

−1 minute

As pupils begin to enter the room, the teacher remains at the threshold. The first instruction they hear is the same instruction every lesson: title's on the board, straight in. Not "good morning, settle down, find your seats" — that is too much, too early, and it cedes the ground.

The greeting is short. It assumes pupils know what to do, because pupils have been taught what to do.

The technique
The starter task is on the board before the first pupil arrives. Pupils enter, see the task, sit, begin. The teacher's role at this stage is to direct, not to explain. Explanation comes after the room is settled.
Scripted language "Morning. Title's on the board. Straight in."

Avoid

"Right, come on then, find your seats, get your books out, today we're doing…"

Use

"Morning. Title's on the board. Straight in."

Deliberate practice 60 seconds · in pairs
  1. One colleague is at the door; the other walks in as a pupil.
  2. The "teacher" delivers the greeting and direction in fewer than ten words.
  3. Repeat with three different "pupils" arriving in succession.
Coach prompt: Was the instruction short and replicable? Did the teacher resist the urge to explain the lesson before the room had settled?
3
The 50% threshold

Move into the room.

0:00

When approximately half the class is in and seated, the teacher transitions from the threshold to the room. This is a deliberate movement, not a drift. The corridor stance has done its work; the room now needs presence.

The teacher moves to a strong position — ideally the front-centre or a lectern — and pauses. The room registers the change. Pupils still arriving will find a class that is already settling.

The technique
Strong position is not a stage. It is a fixed point in the room from which the teacher commands attention without movement. The teacher is upright, weight even, hands free — neither holding a device nor a coffee cup. The body is the lectern.
Deliberate practice 2 minutes · in trios
  1. One colleague is the teacher; two are pupils, one already in, one still entering.
  2. The teacher transitions from threshold to strong position. Deliberate. Unhurried.
  3. Once in position, the teacher waits. No instruction yet.
Coach prompt: Did the move feel decisive, or apologetic? Was the strong position genuinely strong — feet planted, body still — or was the teacher already drifting toward the computer?
4
Ten seconds to be ready

Anchor the standard with virtue praise.

+0:15

From the strong position, the teacher delivers a single line that does two things at once: it sets a clear, measurable expectation, and it praises a pupil who has already met it. The whole class hears both the standard and the model.

The pupil who is praised does not need to be the cleverest in the room — they need to be the one who is ready. Ready often beats clever in the first minute of a lesson.

The technique
Praise must be specific, targeted, and name a virtue. Generic praise ("well done, everyone") teaches nothing because it asks for nothing. Targeted virtue praise asks the rest of the room to match a standard already on display.
Scripted language "You have ten seconds to be ready for learning. Thank you, [Pupil] — already working. That's diligence."
Deliberate practice 90 seconds · in pairs
  1. One colleague is the teacher in strong position; the other is the room — half settling, half not.
  2. The teacher delivers the ten-second line. One sentence. One virtue. One named pupil.
  3. The "pupils" describe what they would have done in the next five seconds.
Coach prompt: Was the praise targeted at a specific pupil and a specific virtue? Did the line set a measurable expectation, or did it drift into hopeful encouragement?
5
The countdown mantra

Same words. Every lesson.

+0:30

Five seconds after the ten-second line, the teacher delivers the mantra. The mantra is the central act of the lesson start — the moment at which the room moves from social to academic.

The words do not change. The pupils have heard them since [Year 7]. They know what comes next. That predictability is the entire point: cognitive load is reduced, attention is freed, and the room responds to a signal it has been responding to for years.

The technique
Three virtues, named in order. A countdown that ends in genuine silence. Heads bowed for the reflection that follows. Do not rush it. Do not fill the silence.
Scripted language "Peacefulness, diligence, and determination in 5, 4, 3, 2, 1."

This is the same mantra rehearsed in Module 1.1. If your delivery is not yet secure, return to that practice slot before continuing.

6
The 30-Second Reflection

Thirty seconds of silence. No exceptions.

+0:40

The countdown ends in silence. The teacher names the virtue for the day, gives a brief reflective prompt, and then says nothing for thirty seconds. Pens are down. Heads are bowed. The room breathes.

This is the most violated stage in the sequence. Teachers under pressure cut it to fifteen seconds, or fill it with their own narration, or skip it altogether on a "busy" day. Every one of those moves erodes the routine. The thirty seconds are not optional.

The technique
Time the silence at first — a watch, a clock, a count. Resist the urge to talk. Resist the urge to police pupils through it; if a pupil is not bowing their head, that is for after, not during. The silence must be held.
Scripted language "Time for reflection. Pens down, heads bowed. Today's virtue is determination. Where will you need it most this lesson?"
Deliberate practice 90 seconds · in pairs
  1. One colleague is the teacher; the other times exactly thirty seconds on a phone or watch.
  2. The teacher delivers the reflection prompt and stays silent for the full thirty seconds.
  3. The timer reports back. Did it feel like thirty seconds to the teacher? It almost never does the first time.
Coach prompt: Did the teacher hold the full thirty seconds, or did they crack at fifteen or twenty? Was the prompt brief and reflective, or did it become a mini-lecture?

Module 1.4 covers the Reflection in full — its pedagogy, its variations, and what to do when pupils struggle to settle into it. For this module, all that matters is the thirty seconds.

7
"Eyes this way"

The single-line transition.

+1:10

The reflection ends with one short line. Not a second mantra. Not a long bridging sentence. Three words.

Scripted language "Eyes this way."

The room comes up. The teacher is still in the strong position. The next instruction follows immediately. Any extended pause here invites drift; any extended speech here dilutes the silence that just ended.

The technique
The transition is a hinge, not a destination. The first mantra has done its work; this is just the closing of the silence and the opening of the work. Three words is enough.
8
The starter task

Two-minute warning. Determination on "go".

+1:30

The starter task is already on the board. Most pupils began it as they entered. The teacher now sets the boundary: two minutes, completed with determination, beginning on the trigger word.

The trigger word is non-negotiable. Pupils do not begin until "go". This is not pedantry — it is the routine that prevents the slow trickle of pupils starting at their own pace, on their own terms.

The technique
Set the time. Name the virtue. Pause. Say "go". Then scan and be seen looking. The task is now running and the teacher has hands free for the register.
Scripted language "You have two minutes to complete the starter, with determination. When I say go. — Pause. — Okay, go."
Deliberate practice 90 seconds · in pairs
  1. One colleague is the teacher; the other is a class half-engaged with the starter.
  2. The teacher delivers the two-minute warning, the trigger word, and scans.
  3. The "pupil" describes whether the boundary felt clear, or whether they were tempted to start anyway.
Coach prompt: Was the pause before "go" actually held, or rushed? Did the teacher scan the room afterwards, or turn straight to the computer?
9
Register & circulate

Be seen looking. Take the register.

+3:00

The room is now working. The teacher takes the register from a position of strength — preferably from the lectern, or moving slowly between the rows. The register is silent administration; it does not need narration. Circulation begins immediately afterwards: spot misconceptions, praise virtue in action, redirect quietly where needed.

The technique
The register is not a private moment between teacher and screen. It is taken in full view of the room. Pupils know that the moment after silent reflection is also the moment when the teacher's eyes are on them most carefully.

The lesson has now begun. The first three minutes have done their work. The remaining fifty-seven are taught from a settled, focused, virtue-anchored room — which is exactly the room every other routine in this Pillar is designed for.

03 · End-to-end practice

Now run the whole sequence.

You have rehearsed each stage in isolation. The final practice is the whole opening, end to end, in real time. This is the practice that closest resembles the real classroom — and the one that exposes whatever has not yet been embedded.

FEATURED PRACTICE · 5 MINUTES · IN QUARTETS

The full ninety-second opening.

Reset the room. One quartet, one teacher, one observer, two pupils. Run the sequence from corridor stance to "okay, go" — without stopping. The observer holds the timeline open and ticks each stage as it lands.

  1. Reset: teacher leaves the room. Quartet sets the scene — board, seats, two "pupils" in the corridor.
  2. Run: teacher executes stages 1 through 8. The observer ticks. No stopping.
  3. Debrief: the observer reads back which stages landed and which were rushed or skipped. Two minutes only.
  4. Rotate and run again. The second attempt is always more secure than the first.
Coach prompt: Which stage was most secure? Which was most rushed? If you had to drop one stage entirely under pressure, which would you protect at all costs — and why?
04 · Common pitfalls

Where lesson starts go wrong.

These are the misuses we see most often when colleagues attempt this sequence without rehearsal.

The soft entry.

The teacher is at the computer when pupils enter, not at the threshold. Without a corridor presence, the room sets its own tone — and the teacher then spends the next ten minutes trying to take it back.

The early explanation.

"Right, today we're learning about…" before the room is settled. Explanation given to a half-attentive room is heard by no one and has to be repeated. Save it for after Stage 7.

The rushed reflection.

Fifteen seconds, twenty seconds, "we'll do a quick one today." The thirty-second silence is the most-violated rule in the sequence. Time it. Hold it.

No starter on the board.

Stage 2 collapses entirely if there is no task to direct pupils to. "Title's on the board, straight in" is a lie if the title isn't there. The board is set before the first pupil arrives.

Talking over the chatter.

The teacher delivers the mantra while three pupils are still talking. The mantra now competes with social chat and loses its weight. Wait. Strong position. Then deliver.

Taking the register too early.

The teacher heads to the computer to log attendance during the reflection or before "go". The room reads this as permission to drift. Register comes after Stage 8, never before.

Apologising for the routine.

"Sorry, can we just have thirty seconds…" The routine is not an imposition. It is the daily standard. Apologetic delivery undercuts the authority the routine carries.

06 · AI scenario tester

A real lesson-start moment.

The full live tool will accept any scenario you describe. The worked example below shows the shape of the response.

Worked example

Scenario tester

Teacher submits
"It's the [Year 9] lesson straight after lunch. The corridor is loud — three other classes are being released at the same time. Pupils are bunching at my door. I haven't had time to put the starter on the board because I was on duty. Two pupils have walked past me and sat down already. I can feel the lesson getting away from me before it's started."
Tool responds

What's going wrong: Three stages have collapsed at once. The corridor presence (Stage 1) is compromised because you were on duty elsewhere. The greeting (Stage 2) cannot direct pupils to a task that isn't there. And pupils are seating themselves on their own terms, which means the 50% threshold (Stage 3) is being set by the pupils, not by you.

Triage, in order:

  1. Get to the threshold. Even now. The corridor presence cannot be recovered for this lesson, but the threshold can.
  2. Hold the door. Slow the entry. "One at a time, please. Title's coming." This buys you forty-five seconds.
  3. Use the slowed entry to put a one-word starter on the board. "Recall: three things you learned last lesson." That is enough. The starter does not have to be polished; it has to exist.
  4. Move to strong position once the room is half-full. Do not skip this stage. The transition matters more than ever today, because the entry was disordered.
  5. Deliver the ten-second line and the mantra at full strength. The disordered entry is not an excuse to deliver an apologetic mantra. Same words. Same weight.
  6. Hold the thirty seconds. Especially today. The silence is what reasserts the lesson over the chaos that preceded it.

The longer-term fix: If the post-lunch corridor is consistently this loud, the issue is not your lesson start — it is the corridor system around it. Raise it with your phase leader. Daily corridor presence by a specific named adult during transitions is an issue for the whole school, not for one teacher.

What not to do: Do not raise your voice at the noisy corridor. Do not skip the reflection because the lesson is "already late". Do not apologise to the class. Each of those moves teaches them that the routine bends under pressure — which is the opposite of the lesson the routine is meant to teach.
