Orientation

Five routines, one purpose.

This module covers mini-whiteboards — the routine that turns "is everyone with me?" into a question that has an answer. It also covers hinge questions: the planned MWB checkpoint that decides whether the lesson moves on.

01 · Why this matters

Every pupil's answer, at once.

Mini-whiteboards solve the problem cold-calling cannot: getting an answer from thirty pupils simultaneously rather than from one named pupil. The teacher reads the room in two seconds and adapts the next instruction.

Dylan Wiliam's research is the anchor. Asking one pupil and watching the others nod tells the teacher nothing about whether the class understands. A mini-whiteboard response from every pupil is "diagnostic information" — the teacher sees who has the answer, who has it partially, and who has it wrong, without anyone in the room being singled out.

If one student gives an answer and everyone agrees, you don't really know what they were thinking. By gathering initial responses from everyone, teachers can draw in a range of views and deepen the discussion.Dylan Wiliam · Effective Questioning in the Classroom

The MWB sequence below is the standard routine. The signal "3-2-1 Show me" is the trigger; the silent thinking time before the trigger is the technique. Both are non-negotiable.

1
Step 1 · Set up the boards

Boards and pens on every desk. Standard.

MW1

The standard at Nishkam School is that all pupils have a mini-whiteboard and a working pen on their desks throughout the lesson, not retrieved at the moment of use. Retrieval consumes thirty seconds of every MWB question; pre-positioning consumes nothing.

The teacher checks at the start of the lesson that boards and pens are out and operational. Where a pupil's pen has dried up, the spare pen is handed out before the first question rather than during it.

Pre-position the kit. A class that retrieves boards mid-lesson will use them less; a class that has them on the desk all lesson will use them often. The investment is in the lesson set-up, not in the moment.
2
Step 2 · Pose the question

Clear instruction. Time limit named.

MW2MW3MW4

The question is posed to the whole class with three things specified: what response is required, what form (a number, a single word, a sentence, a diagram), and what time the pupils have. The instruction is unambiguous; the teacher does not negotiate the form once the time has begun.

MW3 is the discriminating standard: the information asked for must function as a genuine assessment. "Write anything you know about photosynthesis" generates noise; "Write the chemical formula for photosynthesis" generates assessable answers. The question is designed to reveal understanding or its absence.

Scripted language"On your boards. Single word answer. PAUSE What gas do plants release during photosynthesis? PAUSE Thirty seconds, in silence. Go."
3
Step 3 · Silent thinking · the technique

Silence held. Boards hidden. Hesitation noted.

MW5MW6

Pupils write in silence. The standard is that pupils do not show their boards before the trigger word; they keep them flat on the desk or held face-down. This prevents copying and protects the diagnostic value of the technique — the teacher sees what each pupil actually thought, not what each pupil thought their neighbour thought.

While pupils write, the teacher circulates and scans for hesitation. MW5 is explicit: pupils who are delayed in responding, confused, or unable to do the task are identified during this phase, before the reveal. The information is collected silently for use in Step 6 — it is not addressed during the thinking time.

The silence is the assessment. A teacher who narrates ("good, I can see lots of you have started…") destroys the silent thinking that makes the response diagnostic. Circulate quietly; the silence is the work.
Coaching moment3 minutes · in pairs
  1. The partner generates a one-sentence MWB question on a topic the teacher knows.
  2. The teacher delivers the question, names the time, and circulates silently for the full duration.
  3. The partner reports whether the silence was held, whether the teacher narrated during the thinking time, and whether the boards were kept hidden until the reveal.
Coach prompt: Did the teacher fill the silence with encouragement or commentary? Did pupils have full thinking time to retrieve, or were they nudged toward an answer?
4
Step 4 · The trigger

"3-2-1 Show me." Boards raised together.

MW7MW8

MW7 specifies the trigger: "3-2-1 Show me." The countdown is the standard signal; pupils know that on "Show me" every board is raised at the same moment. Simultaneous reveal is what makes the response diagnostic — staggered reveals allow late answerers to see and copy.

MW8 is the discipline. Every board is raised; pupils whose boards are not raised are corrected immediately. The teacher does not begin Step 5 until all boards are visible. A board not raised is the same response as a wrong answer: the pupil could not produce the answer in the time given.

Scripted language"Three… two… one… PAUSE Show me. SCAN"
5
Step 5 · Scan & diagnose · the assessment

Read every board. Acknowledge verbally.

MW9MW10

The teacher scans every board in the room. The scan is unhurried — typically four to six seconds for a class of thirty — and is accompanied by brief verbal acknowledgement: "Good. Yes. Good. Hmm — let's look at that one." The verbal acknowledgement is what tells pupils that their boards have been read, and is the technique that keeps the routine genuine rather than performed.

MW10 is the diagnostic identification: pupils with errors, pupils who hesitated, pupils whose boards are blank, and pupils whose answers appear copied are identified by name in the teacher's mental record. The information is used in Step 6.

Read every board. A scan that skips half the room generates only half the diagnostic information and signals to the unread pupils that their answers are not being taken seriously.
6
Step 6 · Adapt

Reteach, move on, or probe.

MW11MW12

The MWB sequence ends in a teaching decision. MW11 specifies the three options: where misconceptions are present, address them; where pupils have understood, move on; where the majority needs more support, reteach or remodel. The decision is named explicitly to the class so pupils know what is happening and why.

MW12 is the explicit naming. The teacher says "Most of us have this — let's move on" or "Let's reteach this part because four of us went down a similar wrong path." The naming makes the routine feel responsive rather than mysterious; pupils see that their boards have been read and the lesson has adjusted.

Scripted language"Good — most of us have oxygen. PAUSE Three of us wrote carbon dioxide — that's what plants take in. Let's just clarify the difference before we move on."
Coaching moment · full sequence5 minutes · in trios
  1. The trio agrees a topic and one MWB question.
  2. The teacher runs the full six-step sequence. The two "pupils" write on real or imaginary boards — one with a correct answer, one with a deliberate error.
  3. The observer ticks MW1–MW12 as each item lands.
  4. Roles rotate.
Coach prompt: Was the silent thinking time held? Did the teacher name the decision in Step 6, or was the move-on or reteach unspoken?
03 · Hinge questions

The planned MWB checkpoint.

A hinge question is a single MWB question planned in advance, asked at the mid-point of the lesson, designed so the answer reveals whether the class is ready to move on. Wiliam's term: the question on which the rest of the lesson hinges.

The hinge question has three properties. It targets one concept only — not three. Every answer option, including the wrong ones, is plausible — pupils choose the option they think is right rather than guessing among nonsense distractors. And every pupil responds — typically by writing A, B, C, or D on a mini-whiteboard, all revealed on "3-2-1 Show me."

The hinge question is planned before the lesson, not improvised during it. The wrong answers are designed using known misconceptions — Wiliam's example: "If a student is careful, does she need to measure more than once?" Many pupils answer "no", which reveals a misconception about measurement error rather than carelessness. The question is doing diagnostic work because the wrong answer is not a guess but a specific misunderstanding.

A good hinge question gives you diagnostic information. For instance, deliberately put a mistake in a worked example and ask students if it's correct. Knowing that if they say it's wrong, they may have to fix it, creates a "no hiding" culture where thinking is visible.Dylan Wiliam
Coaching moment · design a hinge10 minutes · individual then pairs
  1. Identify one concept from this week's lessons that pupils typically misunderstand.
  2. Write the hinge question with four answer options: A, B, C, D. The correct answer is one; the other three are common misconceptions you have observed.
  3. For each wrong option, write one sentence on what the misconception reveals about the pupil's thinking.
  4. Share with a partner. Run the question on the partner first as a test; revise the options where the partner does not produce the misconception you targeted.
Coach prompt: Are the wrong answers genuinely plausible? Could a pupil who has half the concept choose each one for the right wrong reason?
04 · Pitfalls

Where the MWB routine breaks.

Each pitfall is tagged with the checklist item it puts at risk.

Boards retrieved at the moment of use.Risks MW1

The teacher asks pupils to take out their whiteboards before the question. Thirty seconds lost; momentum lost; the routine becomes onerous and is used less often.

Asking a question that cannot be assessed.Risks MW3

"Write anything you know about…" — every answer is correct in some sense. The technique is wasted because no diagnostic information is produced.

No time limit, or time limit not enforced.Risks MW4

"Take as long as you need" produces uneven responses and stalls the lesson. The time limit is named in advance and held.

Talking through the silent thinking time.Risks MW6

The teacher narrates encouragement during pupils' thinking ("come on, you can do this — most of you have started…"). The silence is the technique. Narration replaces silent retrieval with social pressure.

Boards raised at different moments.Risks MW7, MW8

Pupils raise their boards as they finish, allowing late answerers to copy. The trigger word "Show me" is what produces the simultaneous reveal; pupils raising boards before "Show me" are corrected.

Scanning some boards but not others.Risks MW9

The teacher reads the front rows and skips the back. The unread pupils register that their answers do not matter and stop writing properly on subsequent questions.

No named decision after the reveal.Risks MW12

The teacher reads the boards and silently moves on. Pupils do not know whether the class understood or not, and the routine feels performed rather than diagnostic. The decision is named — "let's move on" or "let's reteach" — out loud.

06 · AI scenario tester

Worked example.

Worked example of a typical tool response.

Worked example

Scenario tester

Teacher submits
"I'm using mini-whiteboards but they feel like a gimmick. The class enjoys writing on the boards but I'm not sure they actually help me check understanding. After 'Show me' I look at the boards and most are correct so I move on, but the next time I question the same content half the class doesn't know it."
Tool responds

Items at risk: MW3 (the question may not be discriminating — most pupils write something correct because the question allows almost any answer), MW9 (the scan may be reading boards too quickly), MW10 (no record of who hesitated or copied), MW12 (no named decision).

What is happening: the boards are being used as a participation tool but not as an assessment tool. The diagnostic information the routine produces is not being acted on, so the gap between "showed me they got it" and "still know it next lesson" is the real measurement.

Across the next two weeks:

  1. Redesign the questions. For each MWB question, ask: could a pupil who has half-understood the concept write an answer that looks correct? If yes, the question is not discriminating. Use Wiliam-style hinge questions with multiple-choice options where the wrong options are common misconceptions.
  2. Slow the scan. Five seconds for a class of thirty, with brief verbal acknowledgement. Read every board, including the back row.
  3. Track hesitation. After each MWB, write the names of pupils who hesitated, copied, or wrote nothing. Return to those pupils with cold-calling in the next ten minutes.
  4. Name the decision. After every MWB, say what is happening next: "moving on" or "reteaching the difference between X and Y." If the decision is unspoken, pupils experience the routine as a check that produced no consequence.

To avoid: using MWBs without redesigning the questions. The routine's diagnostic value comes from the question, not from the boards.
